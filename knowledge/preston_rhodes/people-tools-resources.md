## People

### Nick Cosman
**Who he is:** Online business mentor and content strategist, active in the creator/info-product space. Predates most of the current generation of "online business" influencers.

**What he's known for:** Originator of the **VSSL** (Viral Video Sales Letter) framework — the concept of a video that functions simultaneously as trending/meme-style content AND a long-form sales letter. The built-in friction of a long or unusual video format acts as a buyer-qualifier: if someone watches the whole thing, they're already primed.

**Key framework:**
- Traditional VSL = recorded sales letter, delivered passively
- VSSL = content that's shareable and trending on its own merits, but also carries the full persuasion arc of a sales letter
- Works especially well on TikTok in long-form (10–40 min): nobody else is uploading long videos there, so the algorithm has no competition in that format
- Friction is a feature: "If you make it through the friction, you're just really into the content" — which means you've self-selected as a serious buyer

**Why he matters:** Preston Rhodes cites Cosman as a primary intellectual influence. The VSSL is one of the most sophisticated organic-to-conversion frameworks in the current content economy. Most people are still optimizing short-form for views; the VSSL is optimizing long-form for buyers.

**Relationship to Preston:** Direct mentor/influence. Preston's long-form TikTok strategy descends from Cosman's VSSL framework.

### Sam Ovens
**Who he is:** New Zealand-born entrepreneur who built one of the most influential online consulting/education empires of the 2010s. Founder of **Consulting.com** (formerly SkipperMedia). Known for an ascetic, monk-like lifestyle and extreme personal discipline.

**What he's known for:** The original architect of the "agency/info-model" stack that nearly every online business educator operates within today. His model, developed circa 2015–2019:
1. Free value content to attract leads
2. Low-ticket lead magnet (book, webinar)
3. Application process → high-ticket consulting program ($10K–$25K)
4. Systematic sales process (VSL → call → close)

**Business model:** Info-product + high-ticket coaching. At peak, reportedly generating $30M+/year from Consulting.com selling coaching programs on how to start consulting businesses. Largely pulled back from public life around 2021–2022 — stepped back from social media and much of the business to focus on philosophical and intellectual interests.

**Why the room calls him "The OG":** Before Ovens, high-ticket consulting as an online business category was scattered. He systematized it into a replicable model — VSL, discovery call, close — that became the foundational playbook. Alex Hormozi, Cole Gordon, and essentially every "make money online" coach of the current era can trace their model structure back to Ovens.

**Key insight he contributed:** The "productized service" model — packaging expertise into a defined program with a fixed price and outcome, rather than custom hourly consulting. This made high-ticket online services scalable.

**Why he matters:** Understanding Sam Ovens is required to understand the genealogy of the entire online business education space. If you're studying any of the other people in this document, you're studying derivatives of Ovens.

**Who he is:** Irish online entrepreneur, top-ranked affiliate for **GoHighLevel** globally. Based in the SaaS/creator space.

**What he's known for:** Running arguably the highest-ROI YouTube channel in the GoHighLevel ecosystem:
- ~20,000 subscribers (not a large channel)

- $100,000+/month in affiliate commissions from GHL

**Business model — ultra-niche YouTube affiliate:**
1. Creates deeply specific tutorial content for GHL users (setup walkthroughs, automation flows, snapshot tutorials)
2. YouTube's algorithm learns from extreme content specificity and routes the video only to people actively looking to buy or configure GHL
3. Free snapshots/templates given away in video descriptions — better quality than competitors' paid products — generates email opt-ins
4. 12-hour mega-compilation tutorial videos (combining all individual tutorials + chapter markers) → sustained evergreen signup stream from a single video (600+ signups from one video reported)

**Key insight:** "I have videos with 700 views that get me more signups than most people's viral videos." Views are irrelevant. Viewer-product fit is everything. YouTube's AI optimizes for watch time and engagement, and ultra-niche content achieves both for exactly the right audience.

**Hiring model:** Uses **hiremymom.com** for VAs — predominantly Mormon stay-at-home moms who are reliable, honest, and affordable. Cited as a systematic hiring edge.

**Why he matters:** Declan is the living proof of the ultra-niche small-audience thesis. His numbers are the most credible counter-argument to the "you need viral content to make money" belief. The model is directly replicable in any software-adjacent niche.

**Business model:**

### Frederick Dodson
**Who he is:** Austrian-born author, coach, and consciousness researcher. Prolific writer — over 30 books — mostly in the self-development and consciousness space.

**What he's known for:**
- **Levels of Energy** (the book most cited in the Space) — a consciousness scale applied to human psychological states, moods, and belief systems
- Builds on and extends **David Hawkins'** Map of Consciousness (from *Power vs Force*) but applies it specifically to goal-setting, reality creation, and personal development
- Focuses heavily on the relationship between "energy level" and what you can perceive and achieve — the idea that people at different consciousness levels literally inhabit different experiential realities

**The marketing application (from the Space):**
- Most copywriters target demographics (age, income, job title)
- Dodson's framework adds a psychological state dimension: what energy level is this buyer at?
- Level 200–250 range: "Make money online" crowd — needy, lost, dopamine-driven. Heavy emotional desire, no strategic patience. Copy should be desire-heavy and emotionally charged.
- Level 400+: Self-aware, growth-oriented. Philosophical grounding, intellectual curiosity. Copy should be precise, grounded, and intellectually stimulating.
- Writing for energy level matches the inner state of the buyer, not just their external profile

**Why he matters:** Dodson's energy-level framework is the most differentiated copywriting lens in the Space. Everyone else is writing for personas. This writes for psychological states. For Connor's audience (serious online business builders, not beginners), the Level 400+ copy register is the correct one.

### René Girard
**Who he is:** French-American literary critic and philosopher (1923–2015). Spent decades as a professor at Stanford University in the French literature department. One of the most influential thinkers of the 20th century on desire, violence, and culture.

**What he's known for:** **Mimetic Theory** — the foundational claim that human beings do not generate their own desires organically. They borrow them from other people (models). All desire is triangulated: Subject → Model → Object. You want the object because the model wants it.

**Core concepts:**
- **Mimetic desire**: Desire is learned by imitation, not felt spontaneously
- **External mediation**: The model is distant (historical figures, celebrities) — produces stable aspiration
- **Internal mediation**: The model is proximate (peers, rivals) — produces rivalry, envy, and volatility
- **Mimetic rivalry / the doubles**: When two parties compete mimetically, they become increasingly similar — each mirrors the other until they are indistinguishable
- **The scapegoat mechanism**: Societies discharge accumulated mimetic violence by directing it at a sacrificial victim, restoring temporary order
- **Sacrificial crisis**: When the scapegoat mechanism breaks down, social order collapses

**The Stanford / Peter Thiel connection:** Thiel took Girard's seminar in the 1980s and describes it as the most transformative intellectual experience of his life. Thiel funded the **Imitatio Foundation** (co-founded with Girard in 2008) to apply mimetic theory to the social sciences. Thiel's entire investment thesis — *Zero to One*, "competition is for losers," contrarian bets — is applied Girard.

**Why he matters:** Girard is the intellectual root of multiple frameworks discussed in the Space. Understanding him unlocks Thiel's business philosophy, explains why people buy luxury goods, explains influencer marketing mechanics, explains why copying competitors destroys businesses, and gives Preston's "the algorithm implanted this desire in me" observation a rigorous theoretical foundation.

**Primary text for this context:** *Things Hidden Since the Foundation of the World* (1978) — the most complete statement of the theory. Start with Luke Burgis's *Wanting* for accessibility.

### David Hawkins
**Who he is:** American psychiatrist and consciousness researcher (1927–2012). Student of philosophy and long-term follower of the non-dual teaching tradition.

**What he's known for:**
- **Map of Consciousness** — a scale from 1 to 1000 mapping human psychological and spiritual states, each associated with specific emotions, worldviews, and calibrated "energy levels"
- **Power vs Force** (1995) — the primary text. Uses applied kinesiology as a claimed measurement methodology to assign calibration values to states, people, ideas, and texts
- The scale: Shame (20), Fear (100), Courage (200), Reason (400), Love (500), Enlightenment (700–1000)
- Level 200 is the critical threshold — "power" (constructive, life-affirming states) vs "force" (destructive, fear-driven states below 200)

**Relationship to Dodson:** Dodson builds explicitly on Hawkins' framework. Dodson's *Levels of Energy* applies the same consciousness-scale concept to practical goal achievement and personal development. Hawkins is the origin; Dodson is the applied business/personal development derivative.

**Why he matters in this context:** The energy-level copy framework from the Space traces directly to Hawkins via Dodson. Understanding the original scale (and that "Level 200" is a specific reference to Hawkins' calibration system, not an arbitrary number) explains why the copywriting framework is structured the way it is. The "200–250 crowd" is a specific psychological profile rooted in this map.

### Dave Ramsey
**Who he is:** American personal finance personality, author, and radio host. Founder of Ramsey Solutions. Known for the debt-snowball method and conservative personal finance advice (no credit cards, no debt, emergency funds).

**What he's known for in this context:** The **live call radio show model** — Ramsey built his media empire by taking live calls on air, answering real problems in real time, and packaging the resulting conversations as repeatable content. The calls function as:
1. Ultra-specific, authentic content (real people, real problems)
2. Lead generation (callers become fans and buyers)
3. Social proof (the advice working publicly, in the moment)
4. Infinite content diversity within a single format

**The model Hormozi adapted:** Multiple people in the Space observe that Hormozi's content approach — especially his Q&A formats and "office hours" style content — is the digital adaptation of the Ramsey live-call model. Record the consulting call, post it as content. The specificity of real client problems produces better content than generic advice videos. Los does this as well.

**Why he matters:** Ramsey is the OG of "your consulting calls are your content." Anyone building a personal brand around expertise should understand this model before reinventing it. The Gurley framework applies directly here — Ramsey figured this out in radio decades before it was called content strategy.

### Peter Thiel
**Who he is:** German-American entrepreneur, venture capitalist, and political figure. Co-founder of PayPal (with Elon Musk, Max Levchin, and others), Palantir Technologies, and Founders Fund. First external investor in Facebook ($500K in 2004 for approximately 10% of the company).

**What he's known for:**
- **Zero to One** (2014, with Blake Masters) — the canonical tech startup philosophy text: create something new (0→1), don't copy (1→N)
- "Competition is for losers" — the thesis that competitive markets produce zero profits, and the goal is to build monopolies
- Contrarianism as intellectual method: "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"
- **Girard student**: Took Girard's seminar at Stanford in the 1980s, credits it as the most transformative intellectual experience of his life. Funded the Imitatio Foundation with Girard. His entire investing philosophy is applied mimetic theory.

**Key frameworks:**
- **Definite optimism**: You can build a specific, concrete better future (vs. indefinite optimism: things will probably improve somehow)
- **Secrets**: Every great business is built on a secret — something true that almost nobody believes yet
- **Power Law**: In venture, a single investment can and should return the whole fund. Stop worrying about average performance; optimize for outliers.
- **Monopoly vs. competition**: Monopolies claim to be in competitive markets (to avoid antitrust scrutiny); companies in competitive markets claim monopoly positions (to attract investors). Read through the framing.

**Palantir note:** Named after the seeing stones in Tolkien's *Lord of the Rings*. Government data analytics company. IPO'd September 2020. Thiel is co-founder and early board chairman.

**Why he matters:** Thiel connects the academic theory (Girard) to business practice (Zero to One). He is also the canonical example of someone who identified internet business models before they were obvious (PayPal, Facebook, LinkedIn, Airbnb via Founders Fund) by applying mimetic analysis — betting that crowds were wrong in predictable, mimetically-determined ways.

## Tools & Platforms

### Clay
**What it is:** A data enrichment and outreach automation platform. clay.com

**Core function:** Takes input data (CSV files, LinkedIn exports, company lists) and enriches it with information from 75+ data providers (Clearbit, Apollo, LinkedIn, Crunchbase, etc.) using a spreadsheet-like interface. No code required for most use cases.

**Use cases discussed in the Space:**
- **YouTube subscriber enrichment (Los):** Download a creator's subscriber list as CSV → run through Clay → identify company, title, LinkedIn, and email for B2B outreach from organic content audience
- **Cold outreach segmentation:** Identify outliers in any dataset (e.g., viewers matching specific company sizes or job titles) and personalize messaging to that segment
- **B2B lead gen for local businesses:** Build prospect lists of small credit unions, regional banks, or local businesses → enrich with decision-maker contacts → build outreach sequences

**Why it matters:** Clay is the infrastructure layer for anyone doing B2B outreach in 2025–2026. It bridges organic content audiences with systematic outbound. Declan's model works on affiliate commissions; Los's enterprise consulting model would use Clay to identify the right companies from content viewership data.

**Pricing:** Free tier available. Paid plans scale with enrichment credits.

**What it is:** An all-in-one marketing platform for agencies and small businesses. Includes CRM, email marketing, SMS, funnel builder, scheduling, review management, and a white-label agency program. gohighlevel.com

**Affiliate program structure:**

**The competitive landscape:** Jason Wardrop and Declan O'Reilly are the benchmark names. Wardrop at $600K/month is the ceiling; Declan at $100K+/month is the reachable target. Both prove the model works at dramatically different audience sizes.

**Why it matters:** GHL is probably the single highest-ROI affiliate program available for anyone building content in the agency, online business, or marketing space. The mechanics (recurring, high price, sticky customer) are structurally superior to almost every alternative.

### Open WebUI
**What it is:** An open-source, self-hosted interface for running large language models locally. github.com/open-webui/open-webui

**What it does:**
- Provides a ChatGPT-like interface that runs on your own hardware
- Connects to locally-deployed models (via Ollama) — Llama 3, Mistral, Gemma, and others
- Supports multi-user access, conversation history, document upload, and RAG (retrieval-augmented generation)
- Can be containerized with Docker for enterprise deployment

**Preston's enterprise consulting pitch:**
- Target: companies doing $1M+/month with regulatory or privacy requirements (healthcare, legal, financial services)
- Problem: they need AI assistance but cannot send sensitive data to OpenAI or Google's servers
- Solution: deploy Open WebUI + local models on-premise or on the company's own cloud infrastructure
- Price: ~$75K project fee vs. $250K/year in offshore labor costs
- Requirement: an engineer co-founder to handle containerization and deployment

**Why it matters:** Local LLM deployment is a genuinely underserved enterprise market. Healthcare companies, law firms, and financial institutions cannot use consumer AI products for sensitive data. Open WebUI makes the deployment interface approachable. The business case (replace offshore labor at 70% cost savings) is straightforward for any company that's already spending on that labor.

**Why it matters:** In a conversation full of macro business strategy, this is one of the most specific operational hacks. If you're building any service business with customer support or administrative needs, hiremymom.com is a tested hiring pipeline with lower risk than general freelance platforms like Upwork.

### Creators Corner
**What it is:** A platform and agency co-founded by Jimmy Farley (associated with Champagne Lance) for training TikTok creators and placing them on brand campaigns.

**Two-sided business model:**
- **Creator-side:** Training program that teaches conversion-focused TikTok content (not entertainment/views-optimized content)
- **Brand-side:** Agency that places certified Creators Corner graduates on paid brand campaigns
- Creators are compensated on commission (performance-based), not flat fees

**Market context:** The best converting TikTok creators migrated to TikTok Shop because commissions reward conversion performance directly. This left app companies and traditional brands with a gap — creators who can make content but can't drive sales. Creators Corner fills that gap by training specifically for conversion.

**Why it matters:** As an investment thesis and as a career path. For anyone building in the creator economy, Creators Corner represents the infrastructure layer between creator talent and brand spend. For brands, it's a scalable alternative to UGC marketplaces where quality is inconsistent.

### Kalshi
**What it is:** A regulated prediction markets exchange. kalshi.com

**What it does:** Allows users to trade binary contracts on real-world events — elections, economic data, weather events, sports outcomes. Regulated by the CFTC as a designated contract market (legal in the US, unlike traditional prediction markets that operated in gray areas).

**Why it's mentioned:** Cited in the context of the Space's broader discussion of information, belief, and probability. Prediction markets as a mechanism for converting opinion and informational edge into financial positions.

**Why it matters:** Kalshi represents the intersection of content and information arbitrage with financial markets. For people with genuine domain expertise (understanding the online business landscape better than average, or having early intelligence on market trends), prediction markets are a potential asymmetric bet vehicle.

### Beehiiv
**What it is:** A newsletter platform for creators and publishers. beehiiv.com

**What it does:** Email newsletter publishing with built-in monetization (ad network, paid subscriptions), subscriber management, referral programs, and analytics. Known for creator-friendly pricing and growth tooling.

**Why it's preferred (per Los):** Cited specifically for **local newsletters** as the platform of choice. Beehiiv's architecture prioritizes free-reader-first growth, which is required for the sponsorship monetization model. The ad network connects newsletters with national advertisers; for local newsletters, the direct sponsorship model (selling ad space to local businesses or adjacent software companies) is what works.

**vs. Substack:** Substack is noted as limited because of its paywall friction — the Substack UX is optimized for paid subscriptions, which creates a conversion hurdle that limits free audience growth. Beehiiv's free-tier-first model makes it better for building large, sponsorship-monetizable audiences.

### Notebook LM (Google)
**What it is:** Google's AI-powered research and synthesis tool. notebooklm.google.com

**What it does:** Upload documents, PDFs, URLs, or audio files → Notebook LM builds a "grounded" AI assistant that only reasons from your uploaded sources. Can generate summaries, Q&A, study guides, and podcast-style audio overviews from your source material.

**Why it's mentioned:** Cited as a useful tool for synthesizing long-form content (e.g., a 4-hour X Space transcript) into actionable frameworks. The "grounded" approach (it only uses your sources, not the broader internet) makes it reliable for research on specific documents without hallucination risk.

**Why it matters:** For Connor's use case (turning raw conversations into frameworks), Notebook LM is the correct tool for the synthesis step. Upload the transcript → generate structured summaries → use as input for knowledge file creation.

### Substack
**What it is:** Newsletter and publication platform. substack.com

**Why it's noted (critically):** Mentioned as **limited** due to paywall friction. Substack's model is oriented toward paid subscriptions — the default UX pushes readers toward a paywall. This reduces the addressable market compared to free-first platforms.

**The comparison:** For building a large sponsorship-monetized newsletter (Los's model), free access is critical. Substack's growth mechanics are built for smaller, loyal paid audiences — not the high-volume, free-reader base that sponsors pay CPM rates for.

**When Substack does work:** High-quality writing with an existing audience who will pay. Not the right starting point for building from zero toward sponsorship revenue.

### Medium + LinkedIn (as publishing platforms)
**What they are:** Medium (medium.com) — long-form writing and publishing platform. LinkedIn — professional social network with native long-form article publishing.

**Why they're preferred over Substack:** Both have larger existing audiences with built-in discovery mechanisms. A post on Medium can be indexed by Google and found organically. A LinkedIn article reaches your existing connections without requiring them to take any subscription action. Substack has no organic discovery — you must bring your own audience.

**For content strategy:** Medium and LinkedIn are the correct platforms for written content aimed at business and professional audiences when the goal is **wide addressable market** rather than deep monetization of a small, loyal paid base.

## Books

### Levels of Energy — Frederick Dodson
**What it is:** Consciousness-scale framework applied to human performance, goal-setting, and reality creation. Builds on David Hawkins' *Power vs Force* calibration model.

**Core framework:**
- Human consciousness exists on a measurable scale (adapting Hawkins' 1–1000 model)
- Each level corresponds to specific emotional states, beliefs, capabilities, and life outcomes
- People at lower levels literally cannot perceive possibilities visible to people at higher levels — not because of intelligence, but because of psychological state
- Energy level determines what you attract, what you notice, and what you're capable of building

**Marketing/copywriting application — the key insight from the Space:**
- Writing at the wrong energy level for your audience creates a mismatch that kills conversion
- Level 200–250 audience (desire/fear-driven, "make money online" seekers): Need emotionally charged, desire-heavy, urgency-driven copy. They're running toward something or away from pain.
- Level 400+ audience (reason/self-actualization-driven): Need intellectually grounded, specific, philosophical copy. They respond to precision and integrity, not hype.
- The edge: most copywriters write for demographics. Dodson's framework writes for psychological state — a more accurate model of the buyer's actual decision-making process.

**Why to read it:** It's the least-cited framework in the marketing world with the most practical application. Everyone in "make money online" uses the same emotional triggers because they're targeting the same low-level buyers. If you're targeting operators, executives, or sophisticated builders, you need different copy that most people don't know how to write.

---

### Parallel Universe of Self — Frederick Dodson
**What it is:** Dodson's framework for becoming the future version of yourself by learning to inhabit that identity in the present — before the external circumstances have changed.

**Core premise:** The person you want to become in 10 years already exists as a potential reality. You can begin operating as that person now — in decisions, habits, communication style, and environment — which then draws the external circumstances toward the identity, rather than waiting for the circumstances to create the identity.

**Why Los recommends it:** Directly related to his "objectives vs goals" framework. Instead of setting outcome goals (a revenue number) and working toward them linearly, you identify who you want to be (the "future self") and reverse-engineer what objectives would define that identity's experiences.

**Practical application:** The "10-year morning" visualization from the Space — "what does it feel like, smell like, look like when you wake up 10 years from now?" — is the Dodson parallel universe method in operational form. Start with the experiential identity, work backward to the activities required to sustain it, then work backward to the revenue required to fund those activities.

---

### NLP for Dummies — Various Authors
**What it is:** Introductory text on Neuro-Linguistic Programming — a set of frameworks originally developed by Richard Bandler and John Grinder in the 1970s. The "Dummies" edition is a reference to the accessible introduction format.

**The specific claim from the Space:** "Only 7% of communication is spoken." This is the often-cited breakdown from Albert Mehrabian's 1967 research (later popularized extensively via NLP training): 7% words, 38% tone and voice, 55% body language.

**The context in the Space (Preston):** Applied to AI and LLM limitations — "When you integrate LLMs, you're only making up 7% of being able to connect with someone." The argument: text-based AI communication accesses only the verbal channel. Human trust, connection, and persuasion operate primarily through non-verbal channels that LLMs cannot access.

**Why it matters:** This frames a structural limit on AI-mediated relationships — and simultaneously explains why video content, voice content (Spaces), and face-to-face networking remain valuable and irreplaceable even as text AI becomes ubiquitous. The 93% of communication that isn't words is a moat that AI cannot close through text alone.

---

### Wanting: The Power of Mimetic Desire in Everyday Life — Luke Burgis
**What it is:** The most business-accessible application of René Girard's mimetic theory. Published 2021. Burgis is an entrepreneur who studied with Girard directly and spent years building companies before writing the book.

**Key concepts:**
- **Thin desires vs thick desires**: Thin desires are borrowed from models without personal integration — you want them because someone else wants them. Thick desires are connected to your whole self — your history, relationships, and genuine identity.
- **Mimetic traps in business**: How startup culture, VC funding, and competitive markets are mimetically structured — and how to escape the trap
- **Romantic models**: The people whose desires most shape yours — identifying them is the first step to desire sovereignty
- **Flywheel desires**: Some desires, when pursued, generate more authentic desire. Others are mimetic spirals that intensify without satisfaction.

**Why to read it over Girard directly:** *Wanting* is the bridge between the academic theory and practical application. Girard's primary texts (*Deceit, Desire and the Novel*; *Things Hidden*) are dense literary-critical and philosophical works. Burgis translates them into frameworks, case studies, and actionable questions built for people running businesses.

**Relationship to the Space:** The Girard/Thiel discussion in the Space draws heavily on the framework Burgis popularized. Preston's "the algorithm made me want this" insight is precisely the thin desire / mimetic acquisition dynamic that *Wanting* describes.

---

### Zero to One — Peter Thiel (with Blake Masters)
**What it is:** Thiel's business philosophy, derived from his Stanford CS183 lecture series in 2012. Blake Masters was a student who took detailed notes; the book was co-authored from those notes.

**Core arguments:**
- **0 to 1 vs. 1 to N**: Creating something genuinely new (0→1) is more valuable than copying and improving something existing (1→N, globalization). Most businesses do 1→N. The great ones do 0→1.
- **Competition is for losers**: Perfect competition produces zero profits. Monopoly is the goal. Most businesses lie about being in competitive markets when they're near-monopolies, and vice versa.
- **Secrets**: Every successful business is built on a secret — a truth about the world that almost nobody believes yet. "What valuable company is nobody building?"
- **The Power Law**: In venture capital and in life, outcomes follow a power law distribution. The best investment outperforms everything else combined. Don't diversify — concentrate on the best opportunities.
- **Definite vs. indefinite thinking**: Definite optimists make specific plans and execute them. Indefinite optimists hope things improve without a plan. Greatness requires definite optimism.

**Mimetic content (implicit throughout):** The entire chapter on competition is applied Girard. Thiel never names Girard explicitly in the book, but the analysis of why companies compete mimetically (following each other into the same markets) and how to escape (find a secret, build a monopoly) is Girardian theory translated into business language.

**Why to read it:** It's the clearest single-volume statement of the anti-mimetic business philosophy. Essential context for understanding why Thiel's investments look so different from the herd's — and why the businesses that survive long-term look nothing like their early competitors.

---

### Things Hidden Since the Foundation of the World — René Girard
**What it is:** Girard's most comprehensive statement of mimetic theory, published in 1978 as a book-length interview with two psychiatrists (Jean-Michel Oughourlian and Guy Lefort). Covers mimetic desire, the scapegoat mechanism, sacrificial crisis, and Girard's reading of Christianity as the only tradition to expose (rather than conceal) the scapegoat mechanism.

**Why it's the primary Girard text for this context:**
- The most complete treatment of how mimetic desire operates in groups and societies — not just between individuals
- The scapegoat mechanism is directly relevant to brand strategy, political analysis, and internet culture: cancellations, viral pile-ons, and influencer "beef" in the creator economy are all scapegoat dynamics, whether the participants know it or not
- Girard's analysis of why people converge on shared victims — and why this temporarily restores social order — explains internet drama mechanics that most people observe without being able to explain

**Difficulty level:** Dense. Academic. Written as an extended philosophical dialogue. Start with *Wanting* (Burgis) if you're new to Girard; use *Things Hidden* for depth once the framework is established.

---

### Power vs Force — David Hawkins
**What it is:** Hawkins' original consciousness calibration work, published 1995. Uses applied kinesiology (muscle testing) as a claimed measurement tool to assign numerical values to human psychological states, historical figures, texts, and ideas.

**The Map of Consciousness (summary):**

| Level | State | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 20 | Shame | Near-death, humiliation |
| 30 | Guilt | Destructiveness |
| 50 | Apathy | Hopelessness |
| 75 | Grief | Loss, despair |
| 100 | Fear | Anxiety, withdrawal |
| 125 | Desire | Craving, addiction |
| 150 | Anger | Aggression, hate |
| 175 | Pride | Inflation, denial |
| **200** | **Courage** | **The critical threshold — Power begins here** |
| 250 | Neutrality | Trust, release |
| 310 | Willingness | Optimism, intention |
| 350 | Acceptance | Forgiveness |
| 400 | Reason | Logic, abstraction |
| 500 | Love | Reverence, unconditional |
| 540 | Joy | Serenity |
| 600 | Peace | Bliss, non-duality |
| 700–1000 | Enlightenment | Pure consciousness |

**The 200 threshold:** Below 200, states are "force" — they drain life energy and require external inputs to sustain. Above 200, states are "power" — self-sustaining, constructive, generative. This is the critical dividing line in the framework.

**Relationship to the Space's copy framework:** When the Space references "Level 200–250" as the profile of the "make money online" crowd, they are using Hawkins' calibration directly. This audience is operating from Desire (125) → Anger (150) → Pride (175) → just-cresting Courage (200) — emotionally volatile, externally driven, hungry but undisciplined. Copy for this audience needs to meet them at that energy, not lecture them from Level 400 (Reason).

**Methodological controversy:** Hawkins' kinesiology methodology is not scientifically validated. The claims about "measuring" consciousness with muscle testing are pseudoscientific by conventional standards. The framework is useful in this context as a vocabulary for psychological states, not as empirically validated science.

**Why it still matters:** The metaphor is useful even if the mechanism is unproven. Having a precise vocabulary for "this person is operating from fear vs. this person is operating from reason" is practically valuable for copy, sales, and client work — regardless of whether the specific numbers are calibrated correctly. Dodson's extension of the model makes it more practically applicable than Hawkins' original.
