## Core Thesis

While everyone chases national scale, one city is wide open. The local market is the most undervalued distribution channel in digital marketing — because the internet trained everyone to think national.

The opportunity: become the most recognized person in your city's business, real estate, or professional community. Own that perception for 12–24 months. Deal flow, sponsorships, speaking, consulting, and investment opportunities follow automatically.

> "No one's competing trying to be the biggest person in their area. They just try to take ad space nationally."
> — Los

## Why This Works: The Supply/Demand Gap

### Everyone Is Running the Same Race

Every content creator, every marketer, every newsletter founder is optimizing for:
- National audience
- Maximum total subscribers
- Broad topic coverage
- VC-scale ambitions

The result: national CPMs are saturated and expensive. Local CPMs are effectively abandoned.

### The CPM Arbitrage

| Market | Typical CPM (Meta/Google) | Competition Level |
|--------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| National (US broad) | $8–$25 | Extremely high |
| National (interest targeted) | $12–$40 | High |
| City-level geographic | $2–$6 | Near-zero |
| City + professional interest | $3–$8 | Near-zero |

Running $10/day in retargeting ads to a city of 500,000 people means reaching the same faces repeatedly at a fraction of national cost. You can become omnipresent in someone's feed in a specific city for $300/month.

Local = cheap reach + zero competition + maximum perceived dominance.

## The "Be the Mayor of Your City" Framework

The mental model: you're not trying to be a national influencer. You're trying to be the person that every professional in your city has heard of. Every real estate investor, every business owner, every GP, every attorney — they all know your name.

This is achievable in 12–18 months. Nationally? 5+ years minimum.

### The Four Pillars

1. **Local newsletter** — aggregated content entity with your brand at the top
2. **Local social media presence** — Instagram/TikTok/LinkedIn geo-targeted to your city
3. **Retargeted value content** — "perpetual propaganda" at the city level
4. **Local events and networking** — physical presence amplifies digital presence

## Pillar 1: The Local Newsletter

### Newsletter vs. Email List — Critical Distinction

These are not the same asset:

| Email List | Newsletter |
|------------|------------|
| Personal brand list | Standalone content entity |
| Tied to you personally | Acquirable/sellable asset |
| Monetize via your products | Monetize via ad placements |
| Value dies when you exit | Value survives ownership change |
| Examples: course sales, coaching upsells | Examples: sponsorrhips.com, onlinebusinessowner.com, aisimple.com |

A newsletter is a **media company**. An email list is **a CRM**. Build the newsletter.

### The Morning Brew Model at City Level

Morning Brew's playbook applied to a specific city:

1. **Aggregate the news that matters to local professionals** — commercial real estate deals, city planning approvals, company expansions, funding rounds, key hires, local market data
2. **Add editorial voice** — a hot take, a curated deal, a resource pick
3. **Publish consistently** — daily or 3x/week (daily is the moat)
4. **One niche, one city** — "Austin Commercial Real Estate Weekly" beats "Texas Business" every time

### Real Numbers: The $300K/Year Local Newsletter

The My First Million podcast covered a case study of a single-city newsletter generating $300K/year in revenue. The formula:

- **Audience**: ~10,000–30,000 local subscribers (not hundreds of thousands)
- **Revenue model**: Sponsored placements from non-competing local and regional service providers
- **Advertiser profile**: Software tools (CRMs, ERPs, accounting platforms), local banks, commercial real estate firms, insurance brokers, recruiting firms
- **Rate**: $500–$5,000 per placement depending on send size and format
- **Frequency**: 1–3 placements per send × 3 sends/week = $50K–$300K/year

The key insight: **local advertisers cannot easily reach the concentrated professional audience** you've built. A regional accounting firm cannot efficiently target Austin commercial real estate investors on Meta — the audience is too fragmented. You've aggregated them. That concentration is the product you're selling.

### Newsletter Monetization Stack

| Revenue Stream | Timeline | Typical Range |
|----------------|----------|---------------|
| Newsletter sponsorships | Month 6–12 | $500–$5,000/placement |
| Event sponsorships | Month 8–14 | $2,000–$15,000/event |

### Newsletter Exit: The Acquirable Asset Play

Los sold two newsletters to SEMrush. This is the exit that most operators never see coming.

Properties Los built as standalone entities:
- sponsorships.com — newsletter/resource for newsletter monetization
- onlinebusinessowner.com — small business owner content
- aisimple.com — AI tools for business

Why acquirers buy newsletters:
- **Audience access** — paying for subscribers is cheaper than building from scratch
- **Brand alignment** — the newsletter's editorial voice amplifies the acquirer's brand
- **List quality** — opt-in engaged subscribers > cold paid traffic

Acquisition multiples for niche newsletters typically run 2–5× annual revenue. A newsletter generating $300K/year at 3× = $900K exit. A newsletter at $1M/year at 4× = $4M exit.

The build timeline from zero to acquirable: 18–36 months if executed consistently.

| Large metros (NYC, LA, Miami, Austin) | Instagram | TikTok | High visual culture, lifestyle content consumption |

### Content Strategy for Local Dominance

The content brief is simple: **make locals feel seen**.

- Local market data and analysis ("Orlando CRE transactions were up 34% in Q4")
- Behind-the-scenes access to local deals, buildings, projects
- Introductions to other local operators, investors, founders
- Local events coverage and previews
- "This week in [City]" roundups

Geographic targeting on all paid amplification. Every post is geo-boosted to your city.

### The Paraguay Expat Case Study

Los referenced a case study of someone who moved to Paraguay as an expat and used the following playbook to achieve local prominence within one year:

**The method:**
1. Started contributing free articles and content to the leading local English-language newspaper
2. Covered events, businesses, community news — became a reliable editorial voice
3. Within 6–12 months: backstage access to events, speaking invitations, introductions to the city's top operators and investors

**The mechanics:** When you give a local media outlet useful content for free, you become a trusted contributor. Trusted contributors get access. Access generates relationships. Relationships generate business.

**The modern digital equivalent:**
- Guest-post in local business publications
- Appear on regional podcasts
- Sponsor or co-produce local YouTube content

The timeline is faster with paid amplification, but the organic trust-building dynamic is the same.

## Pillar 3: Retargeted Value Content (The Perpetual Propaganda System)

### What "Perpetual Propaganda" Means

Los's term for a specific type of ad campaign. Not a one-time launch campaign, not a product ad — a continuous, evergreen campaign that places useful/valuable content in front of the same geographic audience repeatedly.

The goal is not a single conversion. The goal is **omnipresence**.

When a local investor, business owner, or professional sees your content in their feed 3, 5, 10 times over 6 months — without you ever knocking on their door — you have already done the relationship work before the first conversation.

### Campaign Architecture

**Step 1: Build the custom audience**

Define your city with a 10–25 mile radius. Stack with professional interests:
- "Small business owners in [City]"
- "Commercial real estate investors in [City]"
- "Entrepreneurs in [City]"

**Step 2: Create the value content library**

Content designed to provide real value, not sell:
- Local market reports (original data or curated)
- Expert perspectives on local trends
- Case studies featuring local businesses/deals
- "State of [City's industry]" content

The content should be good enough that locals share it without you asking.

**Step 3: Run the perpetual campaign**

| Channel | Budget | Format | Objective |
|---------|--------|--------|-----------|
| Meta (Instagram/FB) | $5–$15/day | Video + carousel | Reach + retarget |
| YouTube | $3–$10/day | Pre-roll + in-feed | Retarget engaged viewers |
| LinkedIn (B2B cities) | $10–$20/day | Thought leadership posts | Professional audience |

Total budget: **$300–$500/month**. At city-level CPMs, this buys 50,000–150,000 monthly impressions on a concentrated local audience.

**Step 4: Retarget website visitors and newsletter subscribers**

Anyone who visits your site or opens your newsletter gets added to the retargeting pool. Now your ads follow the people who have already shown interest — amplifying the omnipresence effect at almost no additional cost.

### The Flywheel

```
Valuable content → organic local shares
      ↓
New locals enter audience pool
      ↓
Retargeting ads follow them across channels
      ↓
Newsletter opt-in from repeated exposure
      ↓
Newsletter grows → more social proof → more organic shares
      ↓
[repeat]
```

Each rotation of this flywheel is cheaper and more effective than the last. The audience compounds.

## Pillar 4: Local Events and Networking

Digital presence without physical presence is weaker. The combination is nearly unassailable.

### Event Strategy

**Phase 1 (months 1–6): Attend everything, document it all**

- Every local business mixer, real estate investor meetup, chamber event, founder happy hour
- Document with photos, post recaps to newsletter and social
- You are now the person who covers local events — you get invited to everything

**Phase 2 (months 6–12): Co-produce events**

- Partner with a local venue, co-working space, or sponsor to host a small event (30–80 people)
- The newsletter promotes it (proving the list's value to sponsors)
- Cover the event content in the next newsletter (content creates more content)
- Sponsors pay to be at the event and in the recap — revenue from day one

**Phase 3 (months 12+): Own the annual event**

- Annual summit, investor conference, or industry day
- Sponsorships from national B2B software companies, local banks, regional service firms
- Speaking slots for $5K–$25K each
- Ticket sales from the community you've built

A 200-person local industry event with 3 sponsors at $10K each = $30K in a single day.

## Case Study: Los and the Orlando Private Equity Deal Flow

Los described running this playbook in the context of private equity deal flow in Orlando.

**The setup:**
- Ran geo-targeted ads in Orlando to business owners and investors
- Content focused on business acquisition, PE deal structures, and local market intelligence
- Local awareness created inbound interest from business owners considering selling

**The result:**
- Business owners who'd seen him repeatedly in their feed reached out proactively when they wanted liquidity events
- Warm deal flow from known figures (versus cold outbound to business brokers)
- Relationships with local GPs and family offices who recognized his name

**Why this works for PE specifically:**

Deal flow is relationship-dependent. The dominant constraint in lower-middle-market PE is access to quality off-market deals — not capital. The person who has built trust in a specific market before a business owner decides to sell has an enormous informational advantage.

Running $300/month in local ads for 18 months is $5,400 in total spend. One deal closed creates 100–1,000× return on that investment.

## Newsletter Sponsorship: How to Sell It

### The Advertiser Profile

The ideal sponsor for a local professional newsletter is **not** a local restaurant or retailer. It is:

- National or regional B2B software companies (CRMs, ERPs, accounting software, HR platforms)
- Professional service firms that cannot easily reach your audience (commercial insurance, business banking, corporate law)
- Companies selling to your exact niche (if your newsletter covers real estate, target PropTech, title companies, lenders)
- Adjacent but non-competing education products (masterminds, certifications, conferences)

**Why these companies pay:** Your list is a pre-qualified, concentrated audience they cannot efficiently buy elsewhere. A national SaaS company can buy broad business-owner audiences on Meta all day — but your 8,000 Austin commercial real estate investors are almost inaccessible through any other channel.

### Pricing Formula

```
Monthly newsletter send volume × open rate × placement price per eyeball
```

Simpler rule of thumb: **$30–$50 CPM for a top-of-newsletter placement**, $10–$20 CPM for middle or footer placements, in a niche professional list.

At 10,000 subscribers and 40% open rate (4,000 opens):
- Top placement: 4,000 opens × $40 CPM = **$160 per send**
- 3 sends/week × $160 = $480/week = ~$2,000/month per sponsor
- 3 concurrent sponsors = $6,000/month

At 30,000 subscribers and 40% open rate:
- Same math = ~$18,000/month from 3 sponsors

### How to Pitch the First Sponsor

1. Start with a company whose product you genuinely use and recommend
2. Offer a complimentary placement in your first 3 sends (proof of concept)
3. Share open rate, click data, and any deal flow that resulted
4. Transition to a paid arrangement at $500–$2,000/month
5. Use that first paid testimonial to pitch the next sponsor

The sales pitch: "I have [X] local [audience description] who open every send. You cannot buy this audience this efficiently anywhere else. Here's what the last sponsor saw."

## The Pairing: Why Local Dominance Amplifies These Models

### Private Equity / Business Acquisition

The PE operator's biggest constraint is deal flow — specifically off-market deals with fair pricing. A business owner who knows you, trusts you, and respects your platform is more likely to call you first before going to a broker. One introduced deal worth $1M–$5M in equity more than pays for years of newsletter investment.

### Real Estate

Local dominance means relationships with:
- Developers who share off-market opportunities
- Brokers who call you on pocket listings
- Private lenders who need deal sourcing
- Tenants and investors for syndication

The newsletter is effectively a deal-sourcing engine disguised as a media property.

### High-Ticket Consulting

Being the recognized expert in your city means being the default call when a business has a problem in your domain. Inbound consulting is priced at 2–3× outbound consulting because the prospect has already decided you're the answer before the conversation starts.

### B2B SaaS / Agency

If you're building a software product or agency serving a specific vertical, local dominance in one city lets you:
- Run a low-cost, high-conversion pilot market
- Get warm introductions to the exact buyers you need
- Build case studies from real client relationships
- Expand city-by-city using the same playbook

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Starting nationally**
The urge to grow the list as fast as possible pushes people toward national distribution. Resist it. 5,000 engaged professionals in one city outmonetizes 50,000 scattered national subscribers for most business models.

**Mistake 2: Building a personal brand instead of a media entity**
If the newsletter is "John Smith's Austin Business Digest," you cannot sell it. Build "Austin Business Digest" — with yourself as the recognizable editor, but a brand that can outlive you.

**Mistake 3: Waiting to monetize**
The first sponsor should arrive before 1,000 subscribers. Waiting for "enough" audience to monetize is waiting for permission you don't need. Find one sponsor who wants your 800 subscribers and price it accordingly. This also forces you to take the newsletter seriously as a business.

**Mistake 4: Running ads to cold audiences**
Local ads work when they retarget warm audiences (site visitors, newsletter opens, video viewers). Cold geographic ads to a general population burn budget fast. Build a warm pool first (3–6 months of organic newsletter + social), then retarget that pool.

**Mistake 5: Covering too wide a topic**
"Austin Business News" is too broad. "Austin Commercial Real Estate Intelligence" or "Austin Founder Weekly" or "Orlando Private Equity Deal Digest" is narrow enough to own.

## The Exit Math

A local newsletter that reaches steady-state operations:

| Scenario | Annual Revenue | Acquisition Multiple | Exit Value |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|------------|
| Small market, tight niche | $120K/year | 2.5× | $300K |
| Mid-market city, professional niche | $300K/year | 3× | $900K |
| Large metro, strong community | $750K/year | 3.5× | $2.6M |
| Top-10 metro, established brand | $1.5M/year | 4× | $6M |

Los exited two newsletters to SEMrush. The strategic acquirer was willing to pay a premium because:
1. The audience overlapped directly with their ICP
2. The editorial brand was respected
3. The subscriber list was high-quality and engaged
4. Newsletter operations were systemized (not dependent on a single founder)

Prepare for acquisition from day one: build editorial systems, document processes, keep the brand separate from your personal identity, track detailed subscriber and revenue metrics.

## Summary: The Perpetual Propaganda Stack

```
DAILY:    3x/week newsletter send + 1 social post
ALWAYS:   $10–15/day retargeting to city custom audience
MONTHLY:  1 local event or attendance + contributor content
QUARTERLY: Sponsor outreach, sponsorship renewals
ANNUALLY: Summit/conference + acquisition conversation
```

The flywheel:
1. Newsletter aggregates the audience
2. Social media amplifies reach and brand
3. Retargeting creates omnipresence at low cost
4. Events convert digital relationships to real ones
5. Real relationships generate deal flow and retainers
6. Deal flow and retainers fund more content and growth
7. Media entity becomes acquirable at 2–5× revenue

The total investment in year one: $5,000–$15,000 (ads + tools + event production). The potential outcome: a $300K–$1M+ media asset plus ongoing revenue streams that compound indefinitely.

The competitive moat: the person who runs this in your city for 18 months first holds the position permanently. Catching up requires displacing an established trusted voice — nearly impossible.

Start in one city. Be the mayor.
