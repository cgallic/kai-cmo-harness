## Article

I Googled my name this morning and a Knowledge Panel showed up. The box on the right side of the results. The one Google reserves for people and companies it considers recognized entities.

My AI agent told me how to do it and wrote everything I needed. I just hit submit.

I don't have a Wikipedia page. I don't have a million followers. I'm a guy in Puerto Rico building AI products. And today Google treats me the same way it treats public figures — as a verified node in its Knowledge Graph. Because a month ago I told my agent Kai I wanted one, it built the entire strategy and wrote all the content, and I spent a few hours submitting it.

Here's exactly how that worked, step by step.

## Why a Knowledge Panel Matters More Than Vanity

Most founders hear "Knowledge Panel" and think it's a nice screenshot for social media. It's not. It's **infrastructure for how you show up in AI.**

Google's Knowledge Graph is the database that feeds AI Overviews. Perplexity pulls from it. ChatGPT references it. Every AI system that tries to answer "who is Connor Gallic" checks whether I'm a recognized entity first.

If you're in the graph, you get cited. You get featured. Your name appears in AI-generated answers when someone asks about your industry. If you're not in the graph, you're just another search result competing with everyone else.

A Knowledge Panel means Google has moved you from "collection of web pages" to "known entity." That distinction is the difference between being findable and being authoritative. I wanted that distinction. I didn't want to spend weeks researching how to get it.

So I asked my agent.

## What I Actually Told the Agent

The prompt was simple. I told Kai I wanted a Google Knowledge Panel. I gave it my bio, my company details, my social profiles, my headshot. And I told it to figure out what needed to happen.

The agent came back with a full strategy. Five things needed to exist, and all of them needed to say the exact same thing about me. Kai wrote all the content — bios, descriptions, press copy, Schema code, Wikidata statements. I handled the actual submissions. Here's what we built together.

## Step 1: Wikidata Entry

Kai identified that Wikidata — not Wikipedia — is the structured database that feeds Google's Knowledge Graph directly. Wikipedia is for humans. **Wikidata is for machines.** And the bar for entry is much lower than most people realize.

The agent wrote my entire Wikidata entry — name, description ("American entrepreneur and AI product developer"), aliases, and all the statements I needed to add. Instance of: human. Occupation: entrepreneur. Official website. It also told me which third-party reference URLs to attach to each statement. Not my own site — press articles and professional profiles that independently confirm who I am.

I created the Wikidata account and submitted it all manually. But I didn't have to figure out what to write or how to structure it. Kai handled that. Wikidata doesn't care what you claim about yourself. It cares what other sources corroborate. The agent understood this and mapped out the references before I submitted anything.

## Step 2: Two Press Articles

Kai wrote two press articles for me. One about me personally. One about my company KaiCalls. I submitted them through a PR distribution service and they ran on legitimate news sites — not the New York Times, but outlets Google trusts as independent sources.

Two matters more than one. Google is looking for **independent corroboration from separate sources.** One article could be noise. Two articles from different angles tells the algorithm this entity exists in multiple contexts and is being discussed by independent publishers.

These articles became the reference URLs I cited in the Wikidata entry. The press validates Wikidata. Wikidata feeds the Knowledge Graph. Kai planned the whole chain so each piece fed the next. I just had to execute it in order.

## Step 3: Crunchbase Profile

Crunchbase is one of the databases Google trusts heavily for business entities. Kai wrote the copy for both a founder profile and a company profile. I created the accounts and submitted them. Every detail matched the Wikidata entry and press articles exactly. Same name spelling. Same company name. Same bio language. Same headshot.

Crunchbase alone won't trigger a Knowledge Panel. But combined with Wikidata and press coverage, it adds another **trusted corroborative node** that increases Google's confidence score.

## Step 4: Standardized Every Profile

This is where the agent earned its keep. Kai audited every profile I have online, flagged every inconsistency, and wrote me a standardized bio to copy-paste across all of them.

The problem most people don't realize they have: your LinkedIn says one version of your name, your Instagram bio says a nickname, your website says something slightly different. Three different names means Google sees three different people. The algorithm can't merge you into one entity because you're giving it conflicting signals.

Kai gave me one consistent identity to use everywhere. I went through each platform and updated them:

- **Same full name everywhere** — Connor Gallic, not CG, not Connor G, not a handle
- **Same bio language** — Nearly identical description across every platform
- **Same profile photo** — Identical headshot so even image recognition can corroborate
- **Cross-linked everything** — Every profile points to my main site, my main site points to every profile

I had 10k followers on Instagram and 15k on TikTok already. Those numbers helped because they signal real human engagement with the entity. But followers alone don't trigger anything. I know people with way more followers and no Knowledge Panel because their information is fragmented across inconsistent profiles. **The consistency is what connects the dots, not the follower count.** Kai understood that and prioritized consistency over everything else. I just had to go update the profiles.

## Step 5: Schema Markup on My Websites

Kai wrote the JSON-LD structured data for my about page — the machine-readable code that explicitly tells Google who I am. The most important property is `sameAs`. It literally tells Google "the person on this page is the same entity as this LinkedIn URL, this Wikidata URL, this Crunchbase URL."

Without Schema, Google has to guess whether all your profiles refer to the same person. With it, you're spelling it out in the exact language Google reads natively. Kai also included `knowsAbout` properties — AI, voice technology, entrepreneurship — which tells the Knowledge Graph what topics to associate with my entity.

I dropped the code into my site templates. Copy, paste, done. Kai wrote it, I deployed it.

## 30 Days From Start to Panel

Here's the timeline. About a month ago, Kai gave me the full strategy and wrote all the content within a couple of days. I spent the next two weeks submitting everything — Wikidata entry, Crunchbase profiles, press articles, updating profiles, adding Schema markup.

Then we waited. Google needs time to crawl, reconcile, and build confidence. Jason Barnard — the guy who literally wrote the book on Knowledge Panel optimization — says 3 weeks to 3 months is typical after everything is in place.

Mine showed up today. Roughly 30 days after the last piece was live. I found out because I Googled myself this morning out of habit and there it was.

## The Part That Actually Matters

I didn't spend weeks researching Wikidata submission guidelines. I didn't figure out which databases Google trusts. I didn't write press articles from scratch. I didn't learn Schema markup syntax. I didn't audit my own name consistency across the internet.

Kai did the thinking. I did the clicking. The total time I personally spent was maybe a few hours submitting things the agent had already written and structured for me. Without the agent, I would have spent weeks just figuring out what to do before I even started doing it.

This is what I've been building Kai to be — **my personal marketing CMO.** Not just a chatbot that answers questions. Not just a writing tool. A strategist that researches what needs to happen, builds a plan, writes all the content, and tells me exactly where to put it. I handle the logins and the submit buttons. The agent handles everything else.

A Knowledge Panel used to require hiring an agency or spending weeks doing manual SEO research. Now it requires telling an agent what you want and executing what it gives you back.

## What You Need to Do This Yourself

You need five things:

1. **A Wikidata entry** with every statement backed by third-party references
2. **At least two independent press mentions** — one personal, one company
3. **A Crunchbase profile** matching your other information exactly
4. **Identical name, bio, and photo** across every platform you exist on
5. **Schema markup** on your website with `sameAs` links connecting everything

You can research all of this yourself and write everything from scratch. Or you can hand it to an agent that understands entity building, let it write everything, and spend your time just submitting what it gives you.

Google and every AI system are moving toward entity-based understanding. The founders who get into the Knowledge Graph now are the ones who get cited in AI-generated answers later. The authority compounds. The window is open.

Google your name right now. Tell me what shows up. I'll tell you what's missing.
