# Stop Cramming Context Into Custom GPTs

## Article

I overheard my wife explaining custom GPTs to her boss the other day. It physically hurt me.

Not because she was wrong — she actually did a great job. But because the confusion she was clearing up is the same confusion I see everywhere. People are using custom GPTs, Claude Projects, and ChatGPT Projects completely wrong. They're cramming everything into one place and wondering why the output is garbage.

So let me break this down the way it actually works.

## Custom GPTs Are Tools, Not Workspaces

Think about your garage. You've got a drill, a saw, a sander. Each tool does one thing really well. That's what custom GPTs are. They are **specific tools in your workbench**.

A custom GPT should do one job. Format LinkedIn articles using your SOPs. Write TikTok scripts in your style. Generate Facebook ad copy based on the latest algorithm data. Draft cold emails following a proven sequence.

You name them specifically. "LinkedIn Article GPT." "SEO Blog GPT." "Facebook Ads GPT." Each one gets the frameworks, templates, and rules for that specific task.

Feed your LinkedIn GPT the platform's algorithm updates and your best-performing article structures. Feed your SEO GPT the latest Google ranking factors and your content guidelines. Feed your ads GPT the Meta Ads Library data and your winning creative patterns.

These GPTs become specialized operators. They know the rules of one game and they play it well. That's it. That's all they should do.

## The Mistake Everyone Makes

Here's where people go sideways. They try to cram their company context into the custom GPT. Their brand voice. Their product details. Their client transcripts. Their strategy docs. All of it, shoved into a tool that was never designed to hold it.

This is like taping your entire business plan to a drill. The drill doesn't need your business plan. The drill needs to drill holes.

Custom GPTs have limited context windows. When you overload them with background information plus task instructions plus formatting rules, everything competes for attention. The output gets diluted. The GPT loses focus on the one thing it was built to do.

Stop doing this. There's a better way.

## Projects Are Your Context Engine

Projects — whether in ChatGPT or Claude — are **your workspace**. They're the shed, not the drill. This is where all your context lives.

Create a project for your company. Create one for each client. Create one for yourself. Inside that project, dump everything the AI needs to understand about the subject:

1. Upload your brand guidelines and voice documentation
2. Add product information and pricing details
3. Include transcripts from calls and meetings
4. Drop in strategy documents and SOPs
5. Add performance data and analytics reports

The project holds the **who, what, and why**. The custom GPT handles the **how**.

## The Index File Trick

Here's something most people skip that makes a massive difference. Once you've loaded your project with files, create an index file.

This is a simple document that lists every file in the project with a one-line description of what's inside. "brand-voice.md — Tone guidelines, banned phrases, and writing examples." "q4-analytics.pdf — Revenue data, traffic sources, conversion rates." "client-call-jan15.txt — Discovery call transcript, pain points discussed."

The index file acts like a table of contents for the AI. Instead of burning tokens reading through every document to find what it needs, the AI checks the index first. It finds the relevant file. It reads just that one. Faster responses. Better accuracy. **Less wasted compute**.

## Merge the Two for Real Power

Now here's where it clicks. You take your specialized custom GPTs and tag them inside your projects.

Want to write a LinkedIn article for a client? Open the client project — where all their context lives. Tag your LinkedIn Article GPT — which has all your formatting rules and platform best practices. The GPT writes using the project's context. **Your client's voice, your article framework, one output**.

Want to generate ad copy? Same move. Client project for context. Facebook Ads GPT for execution. The tool uses the workspace. The workspace feeds the tool.

This separation gives you three things:

- **Reusable tools** — One LinkedIn GPT works across every client project
- **Protected context** — Your client data stays in the project, never leaks into shared tools
- **Clean outputs** — Each GPT focuses on its job without context overload

## The Self-Learning Loop

Projects have another advantage that custom GPTs don't. Conversations inside a project are searchable and contained within that project. There's a wall of separation that keeps things clean.

This means you can build on previous work. Reference an old conversation. Refine an approach over multiple sessions. The AI learns your patterns within that project scope.

I did this with my TikTok content. Started a project, fed it my best-performing scripts, ran conversations refining the format, kept feeding winning results back in. After a few rounds, the output was nearly perfect every time. The project became a self-improving content engine because **each conversation compounded on the last**.

You can't do this with standalone custom GPTs. Every conversation starts fresh. There's no memory across sessions. No compounding. No improvement loop.

## The Practical Setup

Here's how to organize this in the next 30 minutes:

1. **Audit your custom GPTs** — Identify which ones are trying to be both tool and workspace. Strip the context out. Keep only the task-specific instructions, frameworks, and format rules.

2. **Create your core projects** — One for your company. One per client. One for personal use. Load each with the relevant context documents.

3. **Build your index files** — One per project. List every file with a short description. Update the index when you add new files.

4. **Test the merge** — Open a project, tag a custom GPT, run a task. Compare the output to what you were getting before. The difference will be obvious.

5. **Feed winners back in** — When a GPT produces great output inside a project, save that output as a reference file. The project gets smarter with every successful run.

## Share the Tools, Keep the Context

One more thing. Custom GPTs are shareable. You can hand your LinkedIn Article GPT to your team, your clients, your community. The tool works for anyone because it's just a framework.

Projects are private. Your client data, your strategy docs, your proprietary processes — all of that stays locked inside your workspace. Nobody sees it. Nobody needs to.

This is how you properly organize your AI setup. Tools stay tools. Context stays context. Merge them when you need output. Separate them when you need security.

Stop trying to make one thing do everything. Build the workbench. Then use the right tool for the job.
