## 1. FTC: Endorsement & Disclosure Rules

**Authority**: FTC Act Section 5; 16 CFR Part 255 (Endorsement Guides, revised 2023)
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission

### What Constitutes an Endorsement

An endorsement is any advertising, marketing, or promotional message for a product that consumers are likely to believe reflects the opinions, beliefs, findings, or experiences of a party other than the sponsoring advertiser. This includes:

- Explicit product reviews and testimonials
- Subtle actions like tagging a brand in a social media post
- AI-generated influencer content or virtual endorser posts
- Any content where a "material connection" exists between endorser and brand

### Material Connection Types (all require disclosure)

| Connection Type | Examples |
|----------------|----------|
| Monetary payment | Flat fees, commissions, affiliate revenue |
| Free/discounted products | Gifted items, press samples, free services |
| Business relationship | Employment, consulting, brand ambassador |
| Family/personal relationship | Spouse, relative, close friend of brand owner |
| Early access | Pre-release products, beta access |
| Contest/prize eligibility | Chance to win, paid appearance opportunity |
| Unrelated compensation | Free products/services unrelated to the endorsed item |

### Disclosure Requirements

**Placement rules**:
- Disclosures must be "clear and conspicuous" -- visible without scrolling, clicking, or expanding
- Must appear BEFORE or alongside the endorsement, not buried at the end
- On social media: must be visible in the initial view (above "See More" fold)
- In video: must appear on-screen for the entire duration of the sponsored segment (visual overlay)
- In audio: must be stated verbally, not just in show notes
- On images: must appear directly on the image, not only in the caption

**Acceptable disclosure language**:
- "Ad" or "Advertisement" -- PREFERRED, most clear
- "Paid Partnership" -- acceptable
- "Sponsored" -- acceptable when unambiguous
- "#ad" -- acceptable ONLY if clearly visible (not buried among 30 other hashtags)

**Unacceptable disclosure language**:
- "Promoted" or "Promoted Stories"
- "Presented by" or "Brought to you by"
- "SP" or "Spon"
- "#ambassador" alone
- Disclosures only in profile bio but not in individual posts
- Vague language that does not clearly communicate a financial relationship

### Liability

All parties face potential FTC Act violations:
- **Advertisers/brands**: liable even if the influencer fails to disclose; must educate and monitor
- **Endorsers/influencers**: personally liable for failure to disclose material connections
- **Intermediaries** (agencies, platforms): liable if they facilitate non-compliant endorsements

### Testimonials & Results Claims

- Testimonials must reflect genuine, current experience of the endorser
- If an endorser's experience is atypical, the ad must clearly disclose what typical results look like
- "Results not typical" disclaimers are NOT sufficient alone -- you must state what the typical consumer achieves
- Endorsers who are presented as experts must actually have relevant qualifications

### Penalties

- Civil penalties up to **$53,088 per violation** (2025 rate, adjusted annually for inflation)
- Each individual ad, post, or communication counts as a separate violation
- FTC can seek injunctions, disgorgement of profits, and consumer redress

## 2. FTC: Truth in Advertising & Substantiation

**Authority**: FTC Act Section 5; FTC Policy Statement Regarding Advertising Substantiation (1984, reaffirmed)
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission

### Core Principles

1. **Ads must be truthful and not misleading** -- no deceptive claims in any medium
2. **Ads must be evidence-based** -- claims require a "reasonable basis" BEFORE the ad runs
3. **Ads must be fair** -- cannot cause substantial injury that consumers cannot reasonably avoid

### Substantiation Standard

The "reasonable basis" depends on:

| Factor | Consideration |
|--------|---------------|
| Type of claim | Health/safety claims require higher substantiation than taste/preference claims |
| Product type | Higher risk products require more rigorous evidence |
| Consequences of false claim | Greater consumer harm = higher bar |
| Cost of substantiation | Considered but does not excuse lack of evidence |
| Expert consensus | What evidence would experts in the field consider adequate |

**If the ad states a specific level of support** (e.g., "clinically proven," "studies show"), you must possess that exact level of evidence.

### Implied Claims

The FTC evaluates both express and implied claims. Examples of implied claims:
- Showing a before/after image implies the product caused the change
- Listing ingredients with known benefits implies the product delivers those benefits
- Using "doctor recommended" implies clinical endorsement

### Comparative Advertising

- Permitted and even encouraged by FTC
- All comparisons must be substantiated
- Must compare on a fair basis (same conditions, same metrics)
- Cannot cherry-pick data to create misleading impression

### 2025-2026 Enforcement Priorities

- **AI capability claims**: FTC has brought 12+ enforcement actions against inflated or unsubstantiated claims about what AI products can do ("Operation AI Comply")
- **Drip pricing / Junk fees**: FTC Junk Fee Rule (effective May 2025) requires advertised prices to include all mandatory fees in live event ticketing and short-term lodging
- **Surveillance pricing**: New York Algorithmic Pricing Disclosure Act (effective November 2025) requires disclosure when personal data is used for individualized pricing
- **Telehealth and GLP-1 claims**: Active enforcement against unsubstantiated weight-loss claims

### Penalties

- Civil penalties up to **$53,088 per violation**
- Injunctions, disgorgement, and consumer refunds
- Corrective advertising may be required

## 3. FTC: Health & Wellness Claims

**Authority**: FTC Health Products Compliance Guidance (December 2022, replacing 1998 Dietary Supplements Guide)
**Enforced by**: FTC (advertising), FDA (labeling)

### Substantiation Requirements

Health benefit claims require **competent and reliable scientific evidence**, defined as:

> Tests, analyses, research, or studies that have been conducted and evaluated in an objective manner by experts in the relevant disease, condition, or function to which the representation relates; and are generally accepted in the profession to yield accurate and reliable results.

### Evidence Hierarchy

| Evidence Type | Acceptability |
|--------------|---------------|
| Randomized controlled human clinical trials (RCTs) | **Required** as general standard |
| High-quality epidemiological studies | Accepted in limited cases where RCTs are infeasible AND experts agree it is an acceptable substitute |
| Animal studies alone | **Not sufficient** for human health claims |
| In vitro (lab) studies alone | **Not sufficient** for human health claims |
| Anecdotal/testimonial evidence | **Never sufficient** as substantiation |
| Expert opinion alone | **Not sufficient** without underlying data |

### Key Rules

1. **Evaluate ALL relevant research** -- cannot cherry-pick studies that support the claim while ignoring contradictory evidence
2. **Expert evaluation required** -- claims assessment should be performed by someone with appropriate expertise in the relevant field
3. **Claim specificity must match evidence** -- if your study shows modest improvement, you cannot claim dramatic results
4. **"Structure/function" claims still need evidence** -- claims like "supports immune health" still require substantiation even if they avoid disease claims
5. **Disclaimer =/= safe harbor** -- stating "results may vary" or "this is not medical advice" does not protect against FTC enforcement if the underlying claim is unsubstantiated

### Common Violations

- Weight loss claims without RCT evidence
- "Clinically proven" without actual clinical trials
- Before/after photos that are manipulated or atypical
- Implying a supplement treats, cures, or prevents a disease
- Using ingredient studies to support product claims (ingredient dosage, formulation, and bioavailability may differ)

### Penalties

- Same FTC penalty framework: up to **$53,088 per violation**
- FTC frequently seeks full consumer refunds for deceptive health claims
- Recent example: NextMed (2025) -- enforcement action for unsubstantiated GLP-1 weight-loss claims and fake testimonials

## 4. FTC: Environmental Claims (Green Guides)

**Authority**: 16 CFR Part 260 -- Guides for the Use of Environmental Marketing Claims (last updated 2012; update pending)
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission

### General Environmental Claims

- **Do NOT use broad, unqualified claims** like "green," "eco-friendly," or "environmentally friendly" -- these are almost always deceptive because no product is entirely without environmental impact
- Any environmental claim must be **specific and substantiated**
- Qualification must be **clear, prominent, and specific** about what environmental benefit is claimed

### Specific Claim Rules

#### "Recyclable"
- Unqualified "recyclable" claim: appropriate ONLY if recycling facilities are available to **at least 60%** of consumers or communities where the product is sold
- If 60% threshold is not met, must qualify the claim (e.g., "Recyclable in some communities -- check locally")
- Must refer to the entire product or package, unless clearly limited to a specific component

#### "Biodegradable"
- Product must completely break down and return to nature within **a reasonably short period** after customary disposal
- Items destined for landfills, incinerators, or recycling facilities should NOT be marketed as biodegradable (landfill conditions prevent breakdown)
- Must have competent and reliable scientific evidence of the timeframe

#### "Compostable"
- Unqualified claim: product must break down in a home composting environment
- If industrial/municipal composting is required, must disclose that qualification
- Must break down at a rate consistent with known compostable materials

#### "Carbon Neutral" / "Carbon Offsets"
- Must substantiate that claimed emission reductions have actually occurred or will occur
- Offsets must be **verified by a reliable third party**
- Must clearly disclose if "carbon neutral" relies on offsets rather than actual emission reductions
- Cannot imply the product itself produces zero carbon if the claim relies on offsets

#### "Free of" Claims (e.g., "BPA-free," "chemical-free")
- Must be truthful -- product cannot contain the substance at any level
- "Chemical-free" is almost always deceptive (all products contain chemicals)
- If the substance was never associated with the product category, the claim is deceptive (implies competitors use it)

#### Certifications and Seals of Approval
- Third-party certification does not automatically make a claim non-deceptive
- Must disclose the basis for the certification
- Seal must not overstate environmental benefit
- If the certifier has a financial relationship with the marketer, must disclose

#### "Made with Renewable Energy" / "Renewable Materials"
- Must substantiate the percentage of renewable energy/materials used
- If the claim covers only a portion of manufacturing, must qualify

### Enforcement

- FTC has pursued cases against companies for "greenwashing" including deceptive "recyclable" claims, unfounded "biodegradable" claims, and overstated carbon neutrality assertions
- Penalty framework: up to **$53,088 per violation**

## 5. FTC: Made in USA Claims

**Authority**: FTC Made in USA Rule (codified 2021, strengthened enforcement); FTC Act Section 5
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission

### Unqualified "Made in USA" Standard

To make an unqualified "Made in USA" claim, ALL of the following must be true:

1. **Final assembly or processing** occurs in the United States
2. **All significant processing** occurs in the United States
3. **All or virtually all** ingredients or components are made and sourced in the United States

"All or virtually all" means the product contains only negligible foreign content. There is no bright-line percentage threshold at the federal level, but the FTC evaluates on a case-by-case basis considering cost of goods, how far removed foreign content is from the finished product, and the importance of the foreign content to the overall product.

### Qualified "Made in USA" Claims

If a product has some but not all domestic content:

- Qualified claims are permitted (e.g., "Assembled in USA from imported parts," "60% US content")
- Qualification must be **clear, conspicuous, and immediately adjacent** to the claim
- Cannot create a misleading overall impression through surrounding text, imagery (e.g., American flags), or design elements that overstate domestic content
- The **overall net impression** standard applies -- even a factually accurate qualifier can be deceptive if the total presentation overstates US content

### California Standard

California allows "Made in USA" when:
- Foreign materials do not exceed **5%** of final wholesale value
- If manufacturer shows inability to source domestically, threshold is **10%** of final wholesale value

### Penalties

- Civil penalties up to **$50,120 per violation** (2025 rate)
- Applies to product labels, catalogs, websites, and all advertising
- FTC has pursued enforcement against DTC brands, supplement companies, and consumer goods manufacturers

## 6. FTC: Free Offers, Trials & Click-to-Cancel

**Authority**: FTC Negative Option Rule (16 CFR Part 425), amended October 2024 ("Click-to-Cancel Rule")
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission

### Scope

The rule applies to ALL negative option programs in ANY medium:
- Free trial conversions to paid subscriptions
- Automatic renewals
- Continuity plans (recurring product shipments)
- Prenotification plans
- Both B2C and B2B transactions
- Online, phone, and in-person signups

### Key Requirements

#### Pre-Purchase Disclosures
Before obtaining billing information, sellers MUST clearly disclose:
- That the consumer will be charged (and the amount)
- The deadline to cancel to avoid being charged
- How to cancel
- The frequency and amount of recurring charges

#### Informed Consent
- Must obtain **affirmative, express consent** to the negative option features specifically
- Consent must be obtained separately from other terms (cannot bury in general T&C)
- Must keep records of consumer consent

#### Simple Cancellation ("Click-to-Cancel")
- Cancellation must be **at least as easy as signup**
- Online signup = online cancellation option REQUIRED
- Cannot require consumers to call, chat, or mail to cancel if they signed up online
- Cannot require consumers to listen to a "save" pitch before canceling (no mandatory retention offers during the cancellation flow as a barrier)
- Cannot impose unreasonable barriers (e.g., long hold times, multiple steps, buried cancel links)

#### Annual Reminders
- For subscriptions with automatic renewal, many states require annual reminders before renewal (California, Colorado, Connecticut, and others)

### Prohibited Practices
- Misrepresenting any material fact about the negative option
- Misrepresenting the cancellation process
- Failing to provide simple cancellation
- Charging consumers without clear disclosure and consent

### Timeline

- Rule finalized: October 16, 2024
- Full compliance deadline: **July 14, 2025** (extended from May 14, 2025)
- Note: The Click-to-Cancel rule was challenged in court; as of early 2026, core FTC Act enforcement against deceptive subscription practices continues regardless, and 25+ states have their own auto-renewal laws

### State Auto-Renewal Laws

Over **25 states** now have auto-renewal and free trial laws, including notably:
- **California** (ARL): Requires express affirmative consent, easy online cancellation, annual reminders
- **Colorado, Connecticut, Maine**: Strengthened protections in 2024-2025 amendments
- **New York**: Requires clear disclosure of auto-renewal terms before purchase

### Penalties

- FTC: up to **$53,088 per violation**
- State AGs: penalties vary by state (California up to $2,500 per violation; some states allow treble damages)
- Private class actions increasingly target subscription compliance failures

## 7. FTC: Native Advertising

**Authority**: FTC Enforcement Policy Statement on Deceptively Formatted Advertising (2015); FTC Native Advertising Guide; FTC Act Section 5
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission

### Core Principle

An ad is deceptive if it misleads consumers about its nature or source. Native ads -- content that resembles editorial or organic content in the medium where it appears -- must be clearly identifiable as advertising.

### Disclosure Requirements

**Placement**:
- Disclosure must appear **in front of or above** the headline of the native ad
- If the focal point is an image or graphic, disclosure may need to appear directly on the image
- Must be visible without clicking, scrolling, hovering, or taking any action
- On mobile: must be legible on small screens

**Language**:
| Acceptable | NOT Acceptable |
|------------|----------------|
| "Ad" | "Promoted" |
| "Advertisement" | "Promoted Stories" |
| "Paid Advertisement" | "Presented by [Brand]" |
| "Sponsored Advertising Content" | "Brought to you by" |
| "Sponsored by [Brand]" (when unambiguous) | "Powered by" |
| | "Featured Partner" |

**Format**:
- Text must be large enough to read easily
- Color contrast must make the disclosure legible (no low-contrast text that blends into the background)
- Font size must be proportional to the surrounding content
- Cannot use design that draws attention away from the disclosure

### Specific Contexts

**Search engine ads**: Must be labeled as ads; search engines have their own labeling but advertisers remain responsible for deceptive content

**Social media sponsored posts**: Must follow both platform requirements AND FTC rules (FTC rules apply even if the platform has its own disclosure system)

**Sponsored articles / advertorials**: "Sponsored Content" label must appear at the top, in a manner that a reasonable consumer would notice before reading

**In-feed ads**: Must be distinguishable from organic content through clear labeling

### Penalties

- Same FTC penalty framework: up to **$53,088 per violation**
- FTC has pursued enforcement against both publishers and advertisers for deceptive native advertising

## 8. FTC: Children's Advertising (COPPA)

**Authority**: Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (15 U.S.C. 6501-6506); COPPA Rule (16 CFR Part 312), **amended April 2025**
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission

### Scope

COPPA applies to:
- Websites and online services **directed at children under 13**
- General audience websites/services that have **actual knowledge** they are collecting data from children under 13
- Mixed-audience sites must implement **age screening before collecting any personal information**

### 2025 Amendment Key Changes (compliance deadline: April 22, 2026)

#### Expanded Definition of Personal Information
Now explicitly includes:
- **Biometric identifiers**: fingerprints, facial patterns, voiceprints, retina/iris patterns, DNA, gait patterns
- **Government-issued identifiers**: state IDs, birth certificates (beyond just SSNs)
- **Geolocation data**
- **Mobile phone numbers** used for parental consent texting

#### Targeted Advertising Restrictions
- **Separate verifiable parental consent** required before disclosing children's personal information to third parties for targeted advertising
- Applies to both first-party and third-party advertising systems
- **Granular consent mechanisms** required -- cannot bundle advertising consent with service consent

#### Data Retention Limits
- **Indefinite retention prohibited** -- personal data must have a defined lifecycle tied to its collection purpose
- Must retain data only as long as "reasonably necessary" to fulfill the specific purpose for which it was collected

#### Security Requirements
- Must maintain a **written information security program** proportionate to size and data sensitivity
- Must designate a security coordinator
- Must conduct annual risk assessments
- Must perform regular testing and service provider oversight

### Parental Consent Methods (approved)

| Method | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Signed consent form | Paper form returned via mail, fax, or electronic scan |
| Credit card transaction | Charge to parent's card as verification |
| Government ID verification | Parent provides ID for verification (delete after verification) |
| Knowledge-based authentication | Questions only the parent could answer |
| Video call verification | Live video call with trained personnel |
| Facial recognition | Match parent's webcam image to government-issued ID (**immediate deletion after verification required**) |
| Text-plus | Text message to parent to initiate consent (where data is not disclosed to third parties) |

### Safe Harbor Programs

- Approved programs must **publicly disclose membership lists**
- Must submit periodic reports to FTC
- Must disclose annual disciplinary actions taken against member operators
- Must provide triennial updates on technological capabilities

### Penalties

- Civil penalties up to **$53,088 per violation**
- FTC has historically pursued large settlements (e.g., $170M against YouTube/Google, $520M against Epic Games/Fortnite)

## 9. FTC: Income & Financial Claims

**Authority**: FTC Act Section 5; Business Opportunity Rule (16 CFR Part 437); Proposed Earnings Claim Rule (January 2025)
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission

### Current Requirements

#### Substantiation
- All earnings claims must be **truthful and substantiated** before being made
- Sellers must possess **written substantiation** backing any earnings claims
- Substantiation must be available to consumers upon request

#### Typical Results Standard
- Claims must reflect **what the typical person is likely to achieve**
- Showing atypical results without disclosing typical outcomes is deceptive
- "Results not typical" or "results may vary" disclaimers are **NOT sufficient** alone -- must state what is typical

#### Expenses Must Be Included
- Earnings claims must account for **both income AND expenses**
- Costs such as product purchases, training, tools, travel, and fees must be subtracted
- Net income (not gross revenue) is the appropriate measure

#### Disclaimer Limitations
- If the disclaimer **contradicts the net impression** of the messaging, it is ineffective
- Disclaimers cannot cure a fundamentally misleading claim
- Small-print disclaimers do not override a bold headline claim

### Specific Prohibited Patterns

- Showing luxury lifestyles as typical outcomes of a business opportunity
- Using cherry-picked top-earner results without clear atypical disclosure
- Income claims in MLM contexts without showing what the majority of participants earn
- "Financial freedom" or "quit your job" claims without substantiation
- Screenshots of income/revenue without context (timeframe, expenses, typicality)

### 2025 Proposed Rule (status: pending/paused)

The FTC proposed in January 2025:
- Expanding the Business Opportunity Rule to cover business coaching and investment opportunities
- Requiring written substantiation for all earnings claims to prospective purchasers
- Mandating record retention for substantiation documentation
- Requiring disclosure of substantiation upon consumer request

Note: This proposed rulemaking was paused under a regulatory freeze in early 2025 but the underlying FTC Act enforcement authority against deceptive earnings claims remains fully active.

### Penalties

- Civil penalties up to **$53,088 per violation**
- Full consumer refunds frequently ordered
- FTC regularly obtains injunctions prohibiting future earnings claims

## 10. FTC: Fake Reviews Rule

**Authority**: Trade Regulation Rule on the Use of Consumer Reviews and Testimonials (effective October 21, 2024)
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission

### Prohibited Practices

| Practice | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Fake reviews** | Creating, selling, buying, or disseminating reviews from people who did not actually use the product/service -- including AI-generated reviews |
| **Fake testimonials** | Fabricated testimonials attributed to real or fictitious people |
| **Insider reviews without disclosure** | Reviews by company employees, officers, managers, or agents that fail to clearly disclose the material connection |
| **Officer/manager reviews** | Officers and managers are specifically prohibited from posting reviews about their own company, even with disclosure |
| **Review suppression** | Using legal threats, physical threats, intimidation, or false public accusations to remove or prevent negative reviews |
| **Buying fake social indicators** | Purchasing fake followers, likes, views, or other social proof generated by bots or hijacked accounts |
| **Selling fake social indicators** | Operating services that sell artificial engagement metrics |
| **Review gating** | Selectively soliciting reviews only from satisfied customers while discouraging dissatisfied customers from reviewing |

### Required Disclosures for Legitimate Reviews

- If a reviewer received the product for free or at a discount, this must be disclosed
- If a reviewer has any material connection to the company (employment, family, business relationship), this must be disclosed
- Disclosure must be "clear and conspicuous" per FTC standards

### Enforcement

- FTC issued **10 warning letters** in December 2025 as its first enforcement action under this rule
- Civil penalties up to **$53,088 per violation**
- Each fake review, each undisclosed testimonial, each suppressed review = separate violation
- Class action lawsuits have been filed seeking **$25M to $500M** in damages for undisclosed endorsements (targeting major brands like Celsius, Shein)

## 11. TCPA: Telemarketing & Robocall Rules

**Authority**: Telephone Consumer Protection Act (47 U.S.C. 227); FCC implementing regulations
**Enforced by**: FCC + private right of action in federal/state courts

### Consent Requirements

#### Prior Express Written Consent (PEWC)

Required for:
- Autodialed calls/texts to cell phones for marketing purposes
- Prerecorded/artificial voice messages to cell phones for marketing
- **AI-generated voices** (FCC clarified February 2024 that AI voices = "artificial or prerecorded")

PEWC must include:
- **Clear and conspicuous written agreement**
- Authorization for **no more than one identified seller** (one-to-one consent, effective January 27, 2025)
- Calls/texts must be **logically and topically associated** with the interaction that prompted consent
- Signature (electronic signatures accepted)

#### Prior Express Consent (verbal OK)

Required for:
- Non-marketing autodialed calls/texts to cell phones (e.g., appointment reminders, account alerts)

### One-to-One Consent Rule (effective January 27, 2025)

- Consent forms can authorize calls from **only one seller** at a time
- The old practice of "lead gen" consent forms authorizing dozens of companies to call is **no longer compliant**
- Each seller must be specifically identified and consented to

### Revocation Rights

- Consumers can revoke consent **by any reasonable method** (including "STOP," "UNSUBSCRIBE," verbal request, email, etc.)
- Businesses must honor revocation within **10 business days** (at most)
- **"Revoke all" rule**: FCC rule requiring consent revocation to apply to all communications from a seller (implementation delayed until April 2026)

### Do Not Call (DNC) Requirements

- Must maintain an internal DNC list
- Must scrub calling lists against the **National DNC Registry** at least every 31 days
- Telemarketing calls restricted to **8:00 AM - 9:00 PM** (recipient's local time)
- Must identify the caller and provide contact information

### Penalties

| Violation Type | Penalty |
|----------------|---------|
| Regulated technology violations (ATDS, prerecorded) | **$500 per violation** (automatic) |
| DNC violations | Up to **$500 per violation** |
| Willful/knowing violations | **Treble damages** ($1,500 per violation) |
| FCC civil penalties (intentional violations) | Up to **$10,000 per call** (TRACED Act) |

### State Mini-TCPA Laws (15+ states as of 2025)

Notable state laws with additional requirements or steeper penalties:

| State | Key Provision |
|-------|---------------|
| **Texas** (SB 140, eff. Sept 2025) | Expands "telephone solicitation" to text/image messages; treble damages via DTPA |
| **Connecticut** (SB 1058) | Requires PEWC for any telephonic sales call; up to **$20,000 per violation** |
| **Florida** | Requires written consent; $500/$1,500 per violation; private right of action |
| **Oklahoma** | Strict consent requirements; state AG enforcement |
| **Washington** | Additional DNC requirements; state AG enforcement |

### 2025-2026 Enforcement Trends

- Class actions spiked in 2025 after courts gained independence to interpret TCPA without deference to FCC guidance (following Supreme Court ruling on Chevron deference)
- AI-generated voice calls are a primary enforcement target
- Lead generation consent practices under heavy scrutiny due to one-to-one consent rule

## 12. CAN-SPAM: Email Marketing (US)

**Authority**: CAN-SPAM Act of 2003 (15 U.S.C. 7701-7713); FTC implementing rules
**Enforced by**: Federal Trade Commission, state AGs, ISPs

### Required Elements for ALL Commercial Emails

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| **From line** | Must accurately identify the sender; email address and domain must be correct |
| **Subject line** | Must accurately reflect message content; deceptive subjects are illegal |
| **Ad identification** | Must clearly identify the message as an advertisement (if not previously consented) |
| **Physical address** | Must include a valid physical postal address of the sender |
| **Opt-out mechanism** | Must include a clear, conspicuous, and functioning unsubscribe mechanism |

### Opt-Out Rules

- Opt-out mechanism must be **easy to find and use**
- Must be able to process opt-outs for **at least 30 days** after the email is sent
- Must honor opt-out requests within **10 business days**
- Cannot require the recipient to log in, pay a fee, or take multiple steps to unsubscribe
- Cannot require personal information beyond email address to process unsubscribe
- Once unsubscribed, **cannot sell or transfer** the email address to another entity for marketing

### Commercial vs. Transactional Emails

| Type | Examples | CAN-SPAM applies? |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| **Commercial** | Promotions, sales offers, newsletters, content marketing | YES -- all requirements apply |
| **Transactional/Relationship** | Order confirmations, shipping notifications, account alerts, warranty info | Partially -- must not be deceptive, but exempt from most CAN-SPAM requirements |
| **Mixed** (both commercial and transactional content) | Treated as **commercial** unless transactional content is the primary purpose |

**A message is "transactional" if its primary purpose is to**:
- Facilitate or confirm a commercial transaction the recipient agreed to
- Provide warranty, safety, or recall information
- Provide information about an ongoing commercial relationship (account status, changes in terms)
- Deliver goods or services under a subscription

### Prohibited Practices

- Using deceptive header information (spoofed From address)
- Using deceptive subject lines
- Failing to include a physical address
- Failing to provide opt-out mechanism
- Ignoring opt-out requests
- Harvesting email addresses (automated collection from websites)
- Dictionary attacks (generating email addresses programmatically)
- Using open relays/proxies to send spam

### Criminal Penalties

CAN-SPAM provides for **imprisonment** for:
- Accessing another's computer to send spam without permission
- Using false information to register for multiple email accounts or domains for spamming
- Relaying messages to disguise origin
- Harvesting addresses via dictionary attack
- Using open relays/proxies without authorization

### Civil Penalties

- Up to **$53,088 per email** in violation (adjusted annually for inflation)
- **No maximum limit** on total fines -- each individual email is a separate violation
- ISPs can sue and recover actual damages
- State AGs can seek injunctions and damages

### 2026 Enforcement Updates

- FTC is leveraging **AI/ML tools** to identify CAN-SPAM violations at scale (analyzing subject lines, sender domains, complaint patterns)
- **Third-party liability** enforced more aggressively: brands are liable for non-compliant emails sent by partners, affiliates, or vendors on their behalf
- "Plausible deniability" for outsourced email marketing is no longer accepted

## 13. CASL: Email Marketing (Canada)

**Authority**: Canada's Anti-Spam Legislation (S.C. 2010, c. 23)
**Enforced by**: CRTC (Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission), Competition Bureau, Office of the Privacy Commissioner

### Consent Types

#### Express Consent (opt-in required)

- Recipient **proactively agreed** to receive commercial electronic messages (CEMs)
- Must be obtained through an **opt-in mechanism** (no pre-checked boxes)
- **Not time-limited** -- valid until the recipient unsubscribes
- Burden of proof is on the sender to demonstrate consent was obtained

Express consent request must include:
- Purpose for which consent is being sought
- Identity of the person seeking consent (and on whose behalf, if applicable)
- Contact information (mailing address + phone/email/web address)

#### Implied Consent (limited, time-bound)

Implied consent exists in these situations:

| Basis | Time Limit |
|-------|-----------|
| Existing business relationship (purchase/transaction) | **2 years** from date of last transaction |
| Existing business relationship (inquiry/application) | **6 months** from date of inquiry |
| Existing non-business relationship (membership, volunteer) | **2 years** from relationship |
| Publicly available email address (e.g., on a website) | Only for messages **relevant to the person's published role** |
| Referral by someone with an existing relationship | **6 months** from referral; may only send **one message** |

### Required Message Elements

Every CEM must include:
1. **Sender identification**: name of the person sending and (if different) the person on whose behalf it is sent
2. **Contact information**: mailing address + at least one of: phone number, email address, or web address -- must remain valid for **60 days** after the message is sent
3. **Unsubscribe mechanism**: functioning opt-out that is easy to use; must be valid for **60 days** after the message is sent

### Unsubscribe Requirements

- Must honor unsubscribe requests within **10 business days**
- Must be free of charge
- Cannot require the recipient to provide information beyond identification/consent to stop receiving messages

### Penalties

| Entity | Maximum Penalty |
|--------|----------------|
| Individual | **$1,000,000** per violation |
| Corporation/Organization | **$10,000,000** per violation |

### Key Differences from CAN-SPAM

| Feature | CAN-SPAM (US) | CASL (Canada) |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| Consent model | Opt-out (can send until unsubscribed) | **Opt-in** (must have consent before sending) |
| Implied consent | Not explicitly addressed | Permitted but time-limited and narrow |
| Penalties | Up to $53,088/email | Up to **$10M** per violation |
| Scope | Commercial email | All commercial electronic messages (email, text, social media DMs) |
| Physical address required | Yes | Mailing address + additional contact info required |

## 14. GDPR: EU Advertising & Data

**Authority**: General Data Protection Regulation (EU) 2016/679
**Enforced by**: National Data Protection Authorities (DPAs) in each EU/EEA member state; lead supervisory authority mechanism

### Applicability to Advertisers

GDPR applies to any organization that:
- Is established in the EU/EEA, OR
- Offers goods/services to EU/EEA residents (even if the company is based elsewhere), OR
- Monitors the behavior of EU/EEA residents (including ad tracking, retargeting, profiling)

### Lawful Basis for Ad Targeting

| Lawful Basis | Use for Advertising | Status (2025-2026) |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Consent** | Behavioral targeting, retargeting, personalized ads | **REQUIRED** -- the only reliable basis for behavioral advertising |
| **Legitimate interest** | Contextual advertising (non-personalized) | Permitted for non-behavioral ads; **NOT sufficient for behavioral advertising** per CJEU rulings |
| **Contract performance** | Service-related communications | Limited to messages necessary to deliver a service the user requested |

**Critical ruling**: The CJEU has clarified that platforms like Meta cannot rely on "legitimate interest" to use off-platform data for targeted ads. **Explicit consent is required for behavioral advertising.**

### Consent Requirements

Consent must be:

| Requirement | Meaning |
|-------------|---------|
| **Freely given** | Must be a genuine choice; no penalties for refusing; no bundling consent with service access |
| **Specific** | Must be granular -- separate consent for analytics, advertising, and social media cookies |
| **Informed** | Must explain what data is collected, by whom, for what purpose, and for how long |
| **Unambiguous** | Requires clear affirmative action (clicking "Accept"); pre-checked boxes are **prohibited** |
| **Withdrawable** | Must be as easy to withdraw consent as to give it |

### Cookie and Tracking Consent

- **No tracking pixels, analytics cookies, or advertising cookies can fire before consent is obtained**
- Cookie banner must include a **"Reject All"** option that is equally prominent as "Accept All"
- No dark patterns: manipulative design to steer users toward accepting cookies is prohibited
- Consent must be documented with **timestamps, source, and IP address**
- Must provide a way to **change or withdraw** consent at any time

### Google Consent Mode v2 (mandatory by 2026)

For agencies managing Google Ads or Analytics for EEA/UK traffic:
- Must use a **Certified CMP** (Consent Management Platform)
- Must send specific consent signals: `ad_storage`, `ad_personalization`, `analytics_storage`, `functionality_storage`
- Non-compliant implementations will lose audience data and conversion tracking

### Data Subject Rights Affecting Advertising

| Right | Impact on Advertising |
|-------|----------------------|
| **Right of access** | Must provide copy of all personal data held (including ad profiles) within 30 days |
| **Right to erasure** | Must delete user from all systems including ad audiences, CRM, retargeting lists |
| **Right to rectification** | Must correct inaccurate data used for targeting |
| **Right to data portability** | Must provide data in machine-readable format |
| **Right to object** | User can object to processing for direct marketing at any time; **must stop immediately** |
| **Right to restrict processing** | Must stop using data for ads while a dispute is resolved |
| **Right not to be subject to automated decision-making** | Profiling for ad targeting may require human oversight or additional safeguards |

### Cross-Border Data Transfers

Personal data cannot be transferred outside the EU/EEA unless:

| Mechanism | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Adequacy decision** | Destination country recognized as providing adequate protection (15 countries as of 2025: UK, Japan, South Korea, Canada, etc.) |
| **EU-US Data Privacy Framework** | US companies self-certified under the DPF can receive EU data (replaced Privacy Shield) |
| **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** | Must use EU-approved contract terms + conduct Transfer Impact Assessment |
| **Binding Corporate Rules** | For intra-group transfers within multinational companies |

**Recent developments**:
- UK adequacy decision renewed December 2025 (valid through December 2031)
- Brazil adequacy decision issued January 2026
- EU-US Data Privacy Framework remains in effect but subject to ongoing legal challenges

### Penalties

| Tier | Maximum | Applies to |
|------|---------|------------|
| **Lower tier** | **EUR 10 million or 2% of global annual turnover** (whichever is higher) | Technical/organizational failures, record-keeping violations |
| **Upper tier** | **EUR 20 million or 4% of global annual turnover** (whichever is higher) | Violations of core principles (consent, lawful basis, data subject rights) |

**Cumulative GDPR fines since 2018**: EUR 5.88 billion (EUR 1.2 billion in 2024 alone)

**Notable enforcement examples**:
- Meta: EUR 1.2 billion (cross-border data transfers)
- TikTok: EUR 530 million (marketing/data processing violations)
- Amazon: EUR 746 million (targeted advertising without proper consent)

## 15. CCPA/CPRA: California Privacy

**Authority**: California Consumer Privacy Act (2018), as amended by California Privacy Rights Act (2020); implementing regulations
**Enforced by**: California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA), California Attorney General

### Applicability

Applies to for-profit businesses that:
- Do business in California AND meet any of:
  - Annual gross revenue > $25 million
  - Buy, sell, or share personal information of 100,000+ California consumers/households
  - Derive 50%+ of annual revenue from selling/sharing California consumer data

### "Do Not Sell or Share" Requirements

- Must display a clear **"Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information"** link on website
- "Sharing" includes transferring personal information to third parties for **cross-context behavioral advertising** (even without monetary exchange)
- Must honor **Global Privacy Control (GPC)** browser signals as valid opt-out (mandatory since 2025)
- Must provide visible **opt-out confirmations** (mandatory starting January 1, 2026)

### Consumer Rights

| Right | Details |
|-------|---------|
| **Right to know** | What personal information is collected and how it is used |
| **Right to delete** | Request deletion of personal information |
| **Right to correct** | Request correction of inaccurate information |
| **Right to opt out** | Opt out of sale or sharing of personal information |
| **Right to limit use of sensitive information** | Restrict use of sensitive data (e.g., precise geolocation, race, health data) |
| **Right to data portability** | Receive data in a portable format |
| **Right to non-discrimination** | Cannot be penalized for exercising privacy rights |

### Advertising-Specific Rules

- Retargeting/behavioral advertising using personal information constitutes "sharing" -- subject to opt-out rights
- Must suppress opted-out consumers from **all remarketing audiences**
- For minors under 16: must obtain **opt-in consent** before selling or sharing data
- For minors under 13: must obtain **parental consent**

### 2026 Regulatory Updates (effective January 1, 2026)

- **Automated Decision-Making Technology (ADMT)**: New rules on profiling, risk assessments, and transparency for AI-driven ad targeting
- **Cybersecurity audits**: Required for high-risk data processors
- **Visible opt-out confirmations**: Mandatory confirmation that opt-out requests have been processed (including GPC signals)

### Penalties

| Violation Type | Penalty |
|---------------|---------|
| Unintentional | **$2,664 per violation** (2025 rate) |
| Intentional | **$7,988 per violation** (2025 rate) |
| Minors' data | Up to **$7,988 per violation** |

- Cure period eliminated as of December 31, 2024
- Private right of action available for data breaches (statutory damages of $100-$750 per consumer per incident)

## 17. FINRA: Financial Services Advertising

**Authority**: FINRA Rule 2210 (Communications with the Public); SEC Marketing Rule (Rule 206(4)-1)
**Enforced by**: FINRA, SEC

### FINRA Rule 2210: Communication Categories

| Category | Definition | Pre-approval |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Institutional communication** | Distributed only to institutional investors | No filing required, but must be supervised |
| **Retail communication** | Distributed to more than 25 retail investors within 30 days | Must be approved by a registered principal; may require FINRA filing |
| **Correspondence** | Distributed to 25 or fewer retail investors within 30 days | Supervisory review required |

### Content Standards

All communications must be:
- **Fair and balanced** -- cannot present only benefits without risks
- **Not misleading** -- no omissions of material facts
- **Not promissory** -- cannot guarantee future results

### Specific Prohibitions

| Prohibition | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Performance projections** | Strictly forbidden for broker-dealers |
| **Guarantees** | Cannot guarantee returns, profits, or specific outcomes |
| **Target returns** | Cannot include "target returns" in private placement communications |
| **Cherry-picked performance** | Must present performance data fairly; cannot show only best periods |
| **Unsubstantiated claims** | All claims about capabilities, track records, or expertise must be provable |

### Testimonials and Endorsements (SEC Marketing Rule)

- Testimonials and endorsements are **now permitted** for investment advisers (changed in 2020 Marketing Rule)
- Must include **clear compensation/conflict disclosure**
- Must include disclosures about whether the person giving the testimonial is a client and whether they were compensated
- Hypothetical or simulated performance results require **proper context and risk explanations**

### Performance Advertising Rules

- Must use **net-of-fees** performance (after deducting advisory fees)
- Must present **1-year, 5-year, and 10-year** (or since inception) performance for standardized periods
- IRR calculations must be consistent with **GIPS (Global Investment Performance Standards)**
- Backtested or hypothetical performance must carry prominent disclaimers and risk disclosures
- Cannot present model/backtested performance as actual performance

### Social Media Rules

- Social media posts by registered representatives are subject to the same standards as any other communication
- Firms must have written supervisory procedures for social media
- "Likes" and "shares" of third-party content may constitute an endorsement requiring compliance review
- AI-generated content must comply with the same standards as human-created content

### 2025-2026 Updates

- **AI-generated communications**: Firms are responsible regardless of whether content is generated by human or AI
- FINRA operating a voluntary **Revised Communication Pilot Program** (July 2025-December 2025)
- Increased scrutiny of **crypto/digital asset advertising** and claims about AI product capabilities

### Penalties

- FINRA can impose fines, suspensions, and bars on individuals and firms
- SEC can impose civil penalties, disgorgement, and injunctions
- Fines typically range from **$5,000 to $500,000+** depending on severity and pattern

## 18. FDA: Drug & Supplement Advertising

**Authority**: Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act; FDA implementing regulations
**Enforced by**: FDA (labeling and promotion), FTC (advertising)

### Prescription Drug Advertising

#### Required Elements
- **Fair balance**: Must present risks and benefits with equal prominence
- **Brief summary**: Must include a "brief summary" of all important risk information from the prescribing information
- **Major statement**: Broadcast ads must include a major statement of risks in audio/video

#### Prohibited Practices
- Omitting or minimizing risk information
- Broadening the indication beyond what is FDA-approved
- Making superiority claims without head-to-head clinical trial evidence
- Using off-label promotion (promoting uses not approved by FDA)

#### 2025 Enforcement Trends
- FDA issued **60 compliance letters** in 2025 (up from 5 before September)
- Focus shifted from TV ads to **HCP-directed websites, sales aids, and newsletters**
- **Reading speed standards** now applied to benefit claims, not just risk disclosures
- **Influencer accountability**: FDA cited endorsers (including celebrities) for violations in drug advertising

### Dietary Supplement Advertising

#### Structure/Function Claims
- Supplements may make "structure/function" claims (e.g., "supports immune health") but NOT disease claims
- Every structure/function claim requires the **DSHEA disclaimer**:
  > "This statement has not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."
- FDA has signaled **flexibility on disclaimer placement** (December 2025) -- no longer required on every panel containing a claim

#### Prohibited Claims
- Cannot claim to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease
- Cannot use language that implies disease treatment (e.g., "lowers cholesterol" = disease claim)
- Cannot imply FDA approval or endorsement

#### Substantiation
- FTC requires **competent and reliable scientific evidence** for all advertising claims (see Section 3)
- FDA requires **substantiation** for labeling claims
- Both agencies coordinate enforcement

### Medical Device Advertising

- Must be truthful and non-misleading
- Cannot make claims beyond the device's cleared/approved indications
- Must include appropriate risk disclosures
- FTC and FDA share enforcement authority

## 19. EU Digital Services Act (DSA)

**Authority**: Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 -- Digital Services Act
**Enforced by**: European Commission (for VLOPs/VLOSEs), national Digital Services Coordinators

### Advertising Transparency Requirements

#### For ALL Online Platforms

Every ad displayed must include:
1. **Clear labeling** that the content is an advertisement
2. **Identity** of the person/entity on whose behalf the ad is presented
3. **Targeting information** -- meaningful explanation of why the user is seeing the ad (main targeting parameters)

#### For Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs: 45M+ monthly EU users)

Additional requirements:
- Must maintain a **publicly accessible ad repository** containing:
  - Content of each ad
  - Identity of the advertiser
  - Period the ad was displayed
  - Number of recipients reached
  - Targeting parameters used
  - Whether the ad targeted specific groups
- Repository must be accessible via searchable API
- First harmonized transparency reports published February 2026

### Prohibited Advertising Practices

| Practice | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Sensitive data targeting** | Cannot target ads based on sexual orientation, religion, race, political opinions, trade union membership, or health data |
| **Profiling children** | Cannot use profiling-based advertising targeted at minors (when platform is aware the user is a minor) |
| **Dark patterns** | Cannot use deceptive design patterns that materially distort users' ability to make free choices |
| **Manipulative techniques** | Cannot use subliminal, manipulative, or deceptive techniques in advertising |

### Platform Obligations for Ad Delivery

- Platforms must provide users with **real-time information** about each ad: "This is an advertisement," who paid for it, and why they are seeing it
- Must provide tools for users to **report** ads that violate the DSA
- Must conduct **systemic risk assessments** related to advertising (for VLOPs)

### Enforcement

- **X (Twitter)**: Fined **EUR 120 million** (December 2025) for violating deceptive design prohibition, ad transparency, and researcher data access rules -- first DSA non-compliance fine
- **TikTok**: Agreed to binding commitments to enhance ad repository transparency (December 2025)
- Potential fines: up to **6% of global annual turnover** for DSA violations
- For systematic violations: up to **1% of global annual turnover** as periodic penalty payments

## 20. ADA: Accessibility Requirements

**Authority**: Americans with Disabilities Act; DOJ Title II Web Accessibility Rule (2024); Section 508; WCAG 2.1
**Enforced by**: Department of Justice, private lawsuits

### Who Must Comply

#### Title II (Government entities)
- All state and local government websites and apps
- Compliance deadline for large entities (50,000+ population): **April 24, 2026**
- Compliance deadline for smaller entities: **April 26, 2027**
- Must meet **WCAG 2.1 Level AA** standards

#### Title III (Public accommodations -- private businesses)
- While no specific regulation yet mandates WCAG compliance for private business websites, courts have consistently held that websites of public accommodations must be accessible
- DOJ has stated that the ADA applies to websites and mobile apps
- **De facto standard**: WCAG 2.1 Level AA

### Requirements for Ad Landing Pages

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Alt text** | All images must have descriptive alternative text |
| **Captions** | Video content must include accurate captions/transcripts |
| **Color contrast** | Minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio for normal text; 3:1 for large text |
| **Keyboard navigation** | All interactive elements must be operable via keyboard |
| **Descriptive link text** | Links must describe their destination (not "click here") |
| **Form labels** | All form fields must have associated labels |
| **Heading hierarchy** | Proper use of H1-H6 for content structure |
| **Responsive design** | Content must be accessible at 200% zoom |
| **No auto-play** | Audio/video should not auto-play; if it does, must provide controls |
| **Error identification** | Form errors must be clearly identified and described |

### Social Media Ad Accessibility

- Alt text for images in ads (available on most platforms)
- Captions for video ads (auto-captions should be reviewed for accuracy)
- Descriptive link text for ad CTAs
- Accessible landing page destinations
- Readable contrast in ad creative

### Lawsuits and Enforcement

- Web accessibility lawsuits in the US average **4,000+ per year**
- Settlements typically range from **$5,000 to $150,000** per case
- Serial plaintiffs and law firms actively file against non-compliant sites
- DOJ consent decrees can impose ongoing monitoring and reporting requirements

### Practical Checklist for Ad Landing Pages

1. Run automated accessibility audit (e.g., WAVE, axe, Lighthouse)
2. Verify all images have meaningful alt text
3. Confirm color contrast meets 4.5:1 minimum
4. Test keyboard-only navigation through the entire page
5. Verify all forms are properly labeled and error messages are clear
6. Add captions to all video content
7. Ensure the page is usable at 200% browser zoom
8. Test with screen reader (NVDA, VoiceOver, or JAWS)

## 21. NAD: Self-Regulatory Standards

**Authority**: National Advertising Division, administered by BBB National Programs (voluntary self-regulation)
**Enforced by**: Industry self-regulation; non-compliance referred to FTC/FCC

### What NAD Does

- Reviews national advertising for **truth and accuracy** across all media
- Accepts challenges from competitors, consumer groups, trade associations, or initiates reviews on its own
- Provides faster, lower-cost alternative to litigation
- Decisions are non-binding but widely respected; non-compliance is referred to federal regulators

### NAD Standards

- Claims must be **truthful and substantiated**
- Performance claims require **competent and reliable evidence**
- Comparative claims must be based on **fair and relevant** comparisons
- Testimonials and endorsements must comply with FTC Endorsement Guides
- Environmental claims must comply with FTC Green Guides

### 2025 Focus Areas

| Area | Details |
|------|---------|
| **AI advertising** | Scrutinizing claims about AI capabilities; requiring substantiation for AI performance claims |
| **Influencer marketing** | Monitoring social media advertising targeting younger audiences |
| **Health/wellness** | Challenging unsubstantiated mood, whitening, and healing claims |
| **Telecom** | Most frequently challenged industry category |
| **Cosmetics/personal care** | Third most frequently challenged category |

### Process

1. Challenge filed (or NAD self-initiates)
2. Advertiser responds with substantiation
3. NAD evaluates evidence
4. NAD issues decision (typically within 60 business days):
   - Claims substantiated
   - Claims modified
   - Claims discontinued
5. Non-compliance referred to FTC for enforcement

### Why It Matters for Advertisers

- NAD decisions can preview FTC enforcement trends
- Being challenged by a competitor at NAD is common and should be planned for
- Maintaining substantiation files for all claims provides protection
- NAD increasingly scrutinizes AI-related and influencer-related claims

## 22. AI-Specific Advertising Rules (2025-2026)

### FTC: AI Capability Claims

- All claims about what AI products can do must be **substantiated with competent evidence**
- "Operation AI Comply" (2023-present): 12+ enforcement actions against inflated AI claims
- AI-generated testimonials and endorsements are subject to the same rules as human-generated ones
- AI-generated reviews are explicitly prohibited under the Fake Reviews Rule

### New York: AI Disclosure in Ads (effective June 9, 2026)

- Requires **conspicuous disclosure** of use of synthetic performers (AI-generated human likenesses) in commercial advertisements
- Applies to all visual or audiovisual advertisements distributed to New York audiences, including online campaigns
- Similar legislation being introduced in other states

### EU AI Act (enforcement began 2025)

- **Prohibited practices** enforceable since February 2025
- **General-purpose AI obligations** since August 2025
- Restrictions on AI-powered ad targeting that uses subliminal, manipulative, or exploitative techniques
- AI systems that profile individuals for behavioral advertising may face transparency and risk assessment requirements

### FINRA / SEC: AI-Generated Financial Communications

- Firms are responsible for AI-generated communications regardless of the technology used
- Same content standards apply (fair, balanced, not misleading)
- Cannot disclaim responsibility because content was "generated by AI"

### FDA: AI in Drug/Device Advertising

- AI-generated promotional materials are subject to the same FDA review requirements
- Endorsers in AI-generated or AI-assisted drug advertising must comply with all disclosure rules

### Best Practices

1. **Audit AI-generated ad content** before publishing for compliance with all applicable rules
2. **Disclose AI involvement** when required by law and as a best practice for transparency
3. **Substantiate AI performance claims** the same way you would substantiate any product claim
4. **Do not use AI to generate fake reviews, testimonials, or social proof**
5. **Monitor state legislation** -- AI advertising disclosure laws are proliferating rapidly

- [ ] Before/after images are genuine and representative

### Children-Targeted Content

- [ ] COPPA compliance verified (under-13 data)
- [ ] Verifiable parental consent obtained before data collection
- [ ] Separate consent for targeted advertising
- [ ] Data retention limited to specific purpose
- [ ] No behavioral advertising to children (DSA, for EU)
- [ ] Age screening implemented for mixed-audience sites
- [ ] Security program in place for children's data
